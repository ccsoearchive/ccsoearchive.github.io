So basically it's just gdkp with no public bidding?

Edit: gdkp with weighted rolling based on bid value. Why not just flat out gdkp it if you want gold value assigned to rolls? Worked fine in icc.

The first reason is that traditional gdkp doesn't work well when there's only one rich person in the group, or if all the rich people are friends with each other.

Example:
poor player only has 500g to spend.
rich player has 200,000g to spend.

Poor player bids 100g.
Rich player bids 200g.
..300, 400, 500.
Poor player stops bidding.
Rich player bids 600g, and wins the item.

Compare single bid. The rich player does not know how much money the poor player has. If they bid less than 500g, the poor player still has a 50% chance to win the item (compared to 0% under traditional gdkp). Suppose the rich player would be willing to bid 8,000g for the item in a bidding war against another rich player, meaning that if they had let their competitor win it for 7,900g (or whatever the next lowest bidding increment is) that cost would not have significantly affected competition for future items by that same player.

If the poor player, or any other player in the raid was actually rich, then the first rich player would have a significant chance to lose the item by bidding low. Example, if they play it "safe" and only bid 1000g, and someone else bids 4000g, under the example formula used in the OP the player who bid 1000g would only be able to roll a 48 at the highest, meaning that they only have a 24% chance to win and if they do win would still have to pay the full 4000g. This gives an incentive to place a bid significantly closer to the true value for the item than if they could be certain of winning at just 600g.

On the other hand, if the rich player decides that they want to conserve gold and only bid 100g even though that would only give them a roll of 1~31 if there was another rich player in the raid who bid 4000g (and therefore a 15% chance of winning RNG if that happened and they chose to match bid), then the poor player can easily match or even exceed that low bid and have a reasonable chance to win the item without being forced out of bidding above 500g. The reason this can work with a tradeoff between risk of losing the item or paying too much is that even 4000g is below the price the player would be willing to pay in a single bidding war with no future strategy considerations.


The second reason is to moderate (but not entirely remove) the advantages of collaboration in groups with a relatively small number of members, where it's reasonable that a majority of the group could have prior association and therefore have built up trust within the subgroup (or even explicit penalties for violating trust, such as loss of guild benefits). This works simply by avoiding bidding wars against other members of the subgroup, if there are no players outside of the subgroup that want the item OR if all players outside of the subgroup are 'poor' and must consider the expected price for future items when determining whether to match the expected value the current item could reasonably take.

In traditional gdkp, the subgroup not only benefits from lower competition (only players outside of the subgroup will drive up the price and there may be none who can use the item) but also can effectively take the strategy of the rich player in the example above by only bidding just high enough to win over poor competitors. A single-bid system encourages more reasonable bidding as the optimal strategy by all members of the subgroup since it doesn't reveal if the other players in the group are poor or even if they've bid at all.


Traditional gdkp only has risks that negatively affect the raid and other players. A single-bid system has risks with positive side effects.

In the end though, it's just a way of distributing items in what's meant to be as fair a way as possible. When GDKP is used in US/EU, one of the most common reasons is to increase overall efficiency to the mutual advantage of both groups of players you describe (either rich, or geared and experienced). But the reasons that it's not commonly used beyond that situation is why, to some people, it has the reputation it has. Fixing those problems is what can allow the general idea of the entire group benefiting no matter what item drops, and without having to worry about 'future obligations' and related drama, to be adopted beyond its mostly niche usage and be used more commonly for progression raiding where wipes are expected to be frequent and the time spent raiding only worthwhile if progression is seen as its own reward regardless of how high or low items go for.

There are cases of GDKP being used for progression in the US/EU, but it's not at all frequent with the multiple bid system that's been used up to now.

Keep in mind a lot of this has to do with the 'planned obsolescence' model ever since the middle of TBC; the first mention of GDKP I know of in Western gaming culture was a post on the WoW forums from around that time describing the 40-person GDKP runs of WoW China among other things. If content is going to become drastically easier in the future it makes little sense to spend too much effort on it in the present; however, if Blizzard goes through with item squishing and/or the mentioned possibility of gear normalization for lower-level instances (similar to challenge modes), it becomes a much more worthwhile proposition to spend effort on current content since it won't actually become less relevant in the future.


Edit: so looking at the goals of people in the raid group. Like you say, some of them will be more experienced, skilled, and better geared than others. Similarly, some players will be richer and this 'progression' (sometimes solely from trading on the auction house) will affect who ends up getting items in the raid. If it's a weekly thing, whether mostly from one guild or even from multiple guilds, then the involvement of wealth will naturally affect the rate of PvE progression. Is this acceptable to people in the raid? If it is, then a GDKP-type system (multiple bid, or single bid like this) has the potential to become more popular.

Basically, if raiding is fun and people enjoy challenge while being able to anticipate future progress due to either becoming more skilled at playing or because of character progression, then it makes sense to use a fair and drama-free loot distribution system. If raiding is NOT fun and people avoid it except at LFR difficulties, then something like this probably wouldn't be used for progression raiding but it seems like it would still help with loot in casual situations (like LFR) where there's basically no chance of failure. That's up to Blizzard and the topic of another thread.
