(By definition they would have the same average points as anyone else, although in practice some people would save points which could have adverse effects on the system.)

If it was just LFR, it honestly wouldn't be that important. But this would probably be necessary if guild alliances are to become more popular, to avoid anyone getting upset at another guild rolling on an item that one's own guild has agreed it would be best if you had.

This is explained in more detail at http://us.battle.net/wow/en/forum/topic/3765088988?page=14#270 (reposted after it was deleted as its own thread because of a 300-word introduction on the challenge posed by SW:ToR)

The way it might work now in a guild alliance: say your guild has 12 members raiding, and another guild has 13. You combined for the day instead of having 5 members with nothing to do. An item drops.

Current system:
Your guild agrees you need the item for future progression. You roll Need and everyone else in your guild passes (no point in rolling greed).
Two people in the other guild roll Need. One rolls lower than you, the other rolls higher and wins it.

Was that really fair? Your guild only had a 33% chance to win the item but contributed 48% of the effort. If the raid ends after this boss, you might feel cheated that two people from their guild rolled and decide not to group with them again, limiting your options.

Single bid system:
Your guild agrees you need the item. You bid 2000g (hidden bid).
Two other people make bids, and it turns out the highest bidder was you so they both decide to match. Since they made lower bids they have less chance to win, and depending on the way it's setup you might have be able to make a roll that can't be exceeded. Example, if they can only roll 1~60 due to low initial, then if you roll over 60 you automatically win no matter if there's 1 person rolling against you or 24.

[...] Who wins the item isn't actually important, because if they win it your guild is compensated for the 48% effort, while if you win their guild is compensated for 52% effort. Everyone is happy and can agree to meet again next week (maybe using LFR to fill slots if people are offline).

The normal GDKP bid system is bad only because it lets a rich player get items with 100% certainty without spending a lot of gold, because they only have to bid 1g (or with minimum increments, maybe 100g) more than what the next person is willing to spend.

With a single hidden bid, either the rich player has roughly the same chance to win as anyone else (for anyone willing to match the top bid), or they have to pay like 10,000g for every item to make sure no one bids.

That's not going to happen often, or to any single player. In one raid you might be forced to drop out because someone bid 10,000g, but on 5 other LFR raids it'll be an item you can't even use, and you'd still get paid 400g each time for that single item not to mention all the other drops the raid has. It would lead to much higher circulation in the economy, but unless you spent that gold on something else you could just turn around and use it for bids in the same LFR raid or future ones. Someone who started out with nothing instead of with 500,000g would get fewer items at start, but there would always be items that are bid for cheaper and it would just mean you get items somewhat slower, not that you can't win anything at all.

The extreme example: for some crazy reason, items are selling for 20,000g each in LFR. Obviously out of your price range. However, with each item that drops you earn 800g which you can use to bid on items. If someone else keeps bidding 20,000g then you have to drop out each time, but after the 6th boss (4 items each) you have 19200g, and after the first drop for the 7th boss you now have 20,000g as well.

If one of the other items that drops is something you can use, then you can match the bid for 20,000g. If you win then you managed to get 1 item the two halves of DS which isn't bad at all, and if you lose the roll you still have 20,000g to use on future bids (or save for a LFR with more reasonable prices).
