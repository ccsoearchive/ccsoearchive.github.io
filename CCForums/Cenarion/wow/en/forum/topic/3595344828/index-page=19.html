Ya know, when I first saw what was happening to Mages (and Frost Mages specifically), I wanted to cry a little. Not really cry, but cry in the sense that a part of me was being lost and it saddened me. What made us survivors, the key talents, were removed. I depended on the talents that kept my Ice Barrier up and running. I depended on the talents that added chill and slow effects to damage abilities. This was what being a Frost Mage was about - surviving long enough to help ensure everyone else survives.

Yes, I realize I give my spec more credit than is due - but when you only do average DPS, you sort of carve out a way to make yourself useful. These changes though, they alter the definition of what I am.

For sheer Nuking ability you went with the Fire Mage. Hard and fast DPS required the Arcane Mage. Crowd Control, Utility and Survivability were the trademarks of your Frost Mage. But will this continue to be the case? Will I still be sought out specifically for a run because I can aid in keeping things under order? Or will I just be another Mage conjuring mana cupcakes?

My Spriest is sort of in the same boat. I do acceptable DPS, can cast damage spells that heal both me and the group, and should the need call for it, I can sacrifice damaging phases and off-heal. This is not likely to continue as Spriest lost Devouring Plague outright, and ceased learning healing spells after level 7. Is my Spriest going to be relegated to the DPS bench where spec doesn't matter, only DPS numbers?

This is what concerns me - if trademarks and defining attributes are removed/altered, what separates us from each other? Why choose one spec over another when they all become fundamentally the same? Yes, yes, that sentence (and probably this whole post) eludes to mountains from molehills and essentially crying about the sky falling - but in this game, it's the inevitable end-thought.

I'm hoping in the future we'll receive some insight as to the intent behind certain changes and what gains are hoped from this. And while I know I'll adapt to these changes and find a way to carve a new niche for myself, I want to be able to do it knowing that it's for a good reason.

Just have to say that a lot of talents for some classes seem pretty generic. I look at the shaman tree and I see maybe 1-2 choices in the entire tree that will remotely benefit me as enhance in any noticeable fashion. Maybe that's the direction talents are going, where my talent choices are between a totem that either prevents interrupts or a totem that prevents movement impairing effects. It was my understanding, however, that the design intent is to make talents actually meaningful, so I shouldn't be able to shrug off half the talent tree as being irrelevant in a PvE environment (outside of the rare situational circumstances). I suppose I was just expecting something more along the lines of rotational changing mechanics that let us customize the way we play our character, not just a few situational CDs.

All that said, I honestly think that reducing the talents to 6 meaningful choices is in concept very viable, but when it comes down to a class having 3 completely different specs (like a shaman) and having -only- 18 choices that are usually heavily biased towards one spec or another, it falls apart. If I were to have the decision, I would have kept separate talent trees for each spec, and custom tailored each talent spec to give a variety of choices that are in fact meaningful.

Without going over specific abilities that may or may not be missing currently (and will likely change anyway):

I like the concept of it. Core "must-have" talents are now just spec-specific learned abilities. There were really only a few true "options" in the cata talent tree, and this system not only expands on the options but focuses on them.

And yes, I can imagine that if you're tank heals or something, there'll be a specific set of options that work best. But iirc you'll be able to change these on-the-fly (possibly with a reagent), so if one night you aren't a tank healer, or the specifics of a fight demand a different set of options, it's easy enough to do.

While in the original talent tree system (and even the cata system) it might seem less restrictive, it was really an illusion; anyone with some experience playing WoW and putting a little thought into their spec would usually hit all the necessary high notes.

I didn't mind the other two talent tree systems we have had to use, but understand and appreciate the streamlining process.

Choice is good, but sometimes too much choice is just artificial/superficial complexity; so much unnecessary mental overhead just to arrive at the same result as everyone else (assuming you didn't just break down and look up a cookie-cutter spec).
