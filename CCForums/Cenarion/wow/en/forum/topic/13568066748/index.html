Cleaning your computer: MAKE SURE YOUR DEFENITIONS ARE UP TO DATE[
Step one: Download R- Kill by following this link: http://www.bleepingcomputer.com/download/rkill/dl/10/
As soon as you download it will activate and turn off most malware threats.

Step Two: Run CCleaner. Under cleaner select “Analyze” Click the “Run Cleaner” option. After that click on “Registry” and “Scan for issues.” After that runs select “Fix selected issues.” When prompted if you want to make a backup select “Yes” if you are paranoid and save to wherever you want it. I usually keep scanning until it finds nothing.

CCleaner issues:

Doing a backup, pressing F8, running "Last Known Good Configuration" and restoring the saved registry will bring you right back if there were an error though. I'll add this part to my guide.

Step Three: Open UVK. Select “System Repair.” There are 4 button on the bottom rightish corner, click import settings and select the file you downloaded from my dropbox. Afterwards click “Run Selected fixes/Apps”
All the Anti Virus programs on the program will update/install themselves and run full scans. They need little user interaction, typically only completing the cleaning portion of the scan at the end.

Step Four: Run Avast! Anti Virus. Update your definitions by selecting settings, update and update. Click scan, from the dropdown select full scan and press start

Step Five: Run SpyBot – Search and Destroy: Click update, click update and allow the update to proceed. Back out of that menu afterwards and select “System Scan” then click “Start a Scan.” This takes a while, so let it go. After the scan is completed go back to the root menu and select “Immunizations.” Check the system and then apply the immunizations.

MISC INFO:

Advanced boot options:

Pressing F8 after restarting/turning your computer on gives you the advanced boot options. From here you have several flavors but the most important is Safe Mode. I do most of my virus removals from Safe Mode as it is a limited system and you have less of a chance of items slipping through the cracks.

———————————————————————————————————————

Always ensure that your Windows installation is up to date. You can ensure this by navigating to:

Control Panel -> System and Security -> Windows Update

Allow Windows to do all of this automagically for the easiest and most secure way to keep your computer up to date.

———————————————————————————————————————

Buy the full versions of Malwarebytes and Avast!. These are two of the best antimalware and antivirus softwares on the markets. Buying them gives you real time protection and better cleaning options. If you need AV protection, I highly recommend these two.

———————————————————————————————————————

Download Firefox or Chrome and use the Adblock addon. This will turn off most of the links that you may have accidentally clicked on to download a majority of the adware etc you have on your computer.

———————————————————————————————————————

Be careful when installing programs. Some adware/spamware programs are packaged in with legitimate software. Sometimes you have to "Decline" the user agreements to bypass downloading these malicious programs

———————————————————————————————————————

If you have a virus such as the FBI Virus the easiest way to remove it is with a rescue disk. There are several flavors out there but I use this one: http://www.hirensbootcd.org/

This CD has an insane amount of tools available and has an antivirus program that you can boot into then run a scan on your hard drive to remove the FBI Virus or whatever is causing you to be unable to boot.

———————————————————————————————————————

Check your error logs: Right click on computer -> Manage -> Event Viewer -> Windows Logs

From here you can check and see where you may be getting crashing errors and such. A useful tool to assist in the repair process

Securing your computer and network:

One of the most effective ways to secure your computer, or network, is through a firewall be it software or hardware based. Thankfully Windows ships with it's own software firewall that does a decent job at blocking malicious traffic. Additionally most user routers include a firewall to some extent. You can lock these down manually to your desires to limit traffic to exactly your specifications.

Beyond that having an effective anti-virus software is crucial for the majority of users. As stated in my tips above I highly recommend obtaining an anti-virus software that is constantly searching for threats. My recommendations for this are Panda, Avast!, Malwarebytes, Spybot S&D. Do some research on effectiveness, let cost (albeit most are extraordinarily cheap now) be a second thought.

Complete periodic backups of your data to an external hard drive or flash drive.

Securing your network:

A wired network is much more secure than a wireless network but most of us have cellphone, tablets, laptops that make this less than feasible. Locking down your wireless connection can be easily completed to give the least amount of opportunity for someone to plunder your epic loots (i.e. data.)

  • Avoid WEP at all costs
  • Use WPA2 with AES
  • Hide your SSID (this is the fancy name of your wireless signal
    • Beyond that you can do some more advanced settings that will slightly increase the security but only if the attacker is not trying too hard to gain access:

    Turn on MAC filtering:     The MAC address for your computer can be found by typing in CMD in your Run bar, and typing: ipconfig /all. Look for the "Physical Address" which looks like A2-F9-3A-3B-9B-5C.

    Turn off WPS: There are exploits available to make that handy little button do bad things.

    Turn on DHCP scope: Limit the amount of IP addresses issues to just the devices you have in the house.

    CCleaner has a Registry cleanup tool. If you have a older machine your registry will likely be a mess. Do not run the tool and tell it to fix all XXX number of errors. That will likely turn out badly.

    If you really want to clean up the Registry - do one section at a time. There are 15 different areas that are cleaned. They are all selected by default. Uncheck everything and start at the top. One section at a time and *backup*. Do a section and test the computer for an hour or two. Repeat.

    If something is broken then go to your C: documents and find the backup and click it to return those settings.

    This is where you need to do some detective work then. Similar to finding a bad addon. You need to go down the list and find what registry entry should not be removed.

    If you use the Registry Tool on a weekly basis it is never a problem. It should be fine on a new system as well. I have seen older systems with near 1000 bad registry entries.

    You can actually notice real world improvements by fixing up a messed up registry. It is a good idea - however it can take some careful work.
