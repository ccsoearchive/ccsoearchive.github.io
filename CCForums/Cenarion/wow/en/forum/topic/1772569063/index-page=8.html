The funny thing is, Blizzard intended for people to be role playing on all server types, as this is a role playing game.

RP servers are only for people who want to be in character at all times. The great majority of role players would not even want to be in character at all times.

The issue is really one of alts for role playing. When you want to just hang out and role play only when you feel like it, you should have that be your main. When you want to be immersed in a world with other people bringing the lore to life, you go to the RP server and log into your RP alt.

People don't even know that's what Blizzard intended. But it's right here if you want proof.

http://us.battle.net/wow/en/game/guide/what-is-wow



ROLE-PLAYING
In World of Warcraft, each player character has a specific set of skills and abilities that define that character’s role. For example, mages are powerful spellcasters who use magic to inflict damage on their enemies from afar but are very vulnerable to attacks. These traits define the role of the mage: hang back, do a ton of damage, and hope to kill the monsters before they reach you.

In a group context, there are three main roles: tank, damage dealer, and healer. A warrior can choose to serve as a formidable “tank,” or protector. Tanks are resilient, and it’s their job to draw the enemy’s attention away from the more vulnerable members of the group. The aforementioned mages make excellent damage dealers. A priest specialized in healing powers may not do as much damage as other classes, but they can play a vital role, keeping the party alive with healing magic. It’s important to note that all classes, regardless of which role they perform, are able to play solo. Some classes are limited in the kind of role they can play: warlocks and rogues, for instance, are strictly damage dealers. Some character classes, like the druid, can capably fulfill all three roles.

Role-play also means that you play the role of a character living in the game’s fantasy world. How much or how little you role-play is up to you; some players construct entire background histories for their characters and adopt unique mannerisms when they’re “in character.” Immersing yourself completely in the fantasy can be a lot of fun, but tastes vary, and it’s perfectly alright if full immersion simply isn’t your style. This kind of role-play is purely optional, and we provide separate Role-Play realms for those who prefer to play in an immersive world.

To summarize: In an MMORPG, you play the role of a unique character in a persistent online world shared by thousands of other players

The problem with RP rules is no one can ever reach a consensus on them. One man's RP is another man's immersion breaker.

For example. I've been told by one RPer that this name doesn't meet RP guidelines in their opinion and they would report me. However, others think it does because "downsizing" is a business term and fit the goblin persona.

I've ran into other scenarios where some RPers like to "godmode". This upsets other members of the RPer community.

Then there is Mature RP. For some, this is acceptable. For others, this is simply cybering.

For all the unity the RP community claims, they are only unified when people are RP-ing the way they think RP should be done. When you actually get inside an RP community, you see they have their power struggles and arguments because someone is RP-ing their character in a way that someone else disagrees with. Because of this, there can never be a set of enforceable RP rules.

I think RP in a computer-based world is fundamentally flawed to begin with, to be honest.

A big part of what makes something like a tabletop RPG fun is that the rules, while clearly defined, can always be bent or broken if it would make the game better. That's what a Game Master/ Dungeon Master is supposed to do, not just blindly enforce the mechanics as written. However, that blind enforcement is all a computer is capable of.

Go read any good WoW-inspired webcomic, or dig up the old Flintlocke comics on GameSpy. Virtually every strip will involve the characters doing things that simply can't be done in the game (such as Flintlocke's tendency to use teammates or small woodland creatures as ammo or weapons). You can't "think outside the box" like this in an MMO, because it would have to be programmed, balanced, and tweaked to the point where it was just another skill available to everyone of your class.

Think about Hunters' snake trap and trap launcher abilites. They are throwing a spring loaded trap full of live snakes at someone! That should be awesome! But it has to be balanced, debugged, and incorporated into the game in such a way as the computer can handle it every time. So it's no more remarkable than a fireball spell.

I'm not trying to chase RP out of WoW, but I really wish folks fighting for it would think about what they're fighting for. Names and speech are a pretty trivial part of any game, RP or not. If the gameplay is set in stone (as it has to be for an MMO), then there's really no room to RP in the first place.


This is why we have the ability to create our own emotes and make things as epic as we want them to be. Imaginative thinking is the key component to RP. Game mechanics, while a good factor and can decide some RP encounters, are usually not taken into consideration for most RP ordeals.
