I have never really been a big fan of dailies. There's infinitely easier ways to make gold. From a rewards perspective though (rep, mounts, gear) dailies can be very fulfilling. My only real issue with them comes from the repetitiveness of doing them. I liked the molten front dailies because they offered great rewards for spending about 30-45 minutes a day questing. The quests had a chance to be different each day, and after doing them for so many days you unlocked additional quest hubs.

This kept the process of doing the dailies 'interesting' and for someone like me who is strictly a pvp player, the whole thing was a great world pvp front (brought back memories of Isle of Quel'Danas world pvp, even if the MF is a much darker and hellish version). There were days when I could just engage kids in pvp and the quests would almost complete themselves. It was pretty awesome.

The Molten Front proved that Blizzard can create content that incites conflict between the horde and alliance (world pvp) without forcing it on a schedule (Wintergrasp, Tol Barad). I would love to see areas like the Molten Front added in new expansions as well as for lower levels in the game.

If I could make a suggestion though: Even the Molten Front dailies got repetitive. Maybe we could see a greater variety between the quests given, much like the fishing dailies post Cata. Maybe we could see old leveling zones (pre 60 especially) where we experience the change to an area through a set of daily quests which reward something useful.

An example would be instead of players seeing SS/TM/Hillsbrad as they are now, we could initially come upon that area at level 20ish, and see them as they used to be. Through the process of dailies we could see the area change from what it was back then to what it is now (using phased tech of course).

There are a lot of examples of areas of the world that have changed over time, and it would be great if players could experience some of that change as they level.

I still don't have all the Wrath patterns because I just can't keep doing it day after day after day.     That being said, why are you harping on the OP's use of the term "bad design"?


    Because language is important, and also, because it's often used in the phrase, "That's just bad design." to justify why a mechanic or feature is undesirable to the poster in question. It presupposes the correctness of an opinion which may not, in fact, be correct. It also tells me nothing useful, except "I don't like it", but it makes, "I don't like it." sound more erudite, knowledgeable and sophisticated. It still boils down to, "I don't like it.", which isn't particularly useful without a context.

    We still appreciate the feedback we receive, but when it comes to feedback, details are important. That's one of the reasons we solicit thoughtful, constructive feedback: because it's more useful than blanket naysaying.
