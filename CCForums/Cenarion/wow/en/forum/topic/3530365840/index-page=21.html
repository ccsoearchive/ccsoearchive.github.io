I loved the Silithus end game. The farming of the twilight texts and cultist attire to summon the bosses which would give you the next tier up item to summon another boss and next tier up etc... ultimately leading to mini raid bosses was great.

I thought it was a well gated system in that it was an immense amount of work if you did it individually but it encouraged group play and pooling of resources (since the rep was shared anyway). The higher you took it the the better the rewards from very well itemised blue rewards or epic rewards from the higher bosses.

It all added up to flexibility, you could have 1, 2 or up to 5 man teams go out and farm the items to summon the bosses then come together as a raid and actually defeat the higher end bosses.

The best part about it was that it was all boe, so you could sell/AH any or all of the items if that was your thing too (so raid guilds could trawl the AH for items summon a bunch of bosses then go raid).

If you could add in dailies on the side, you probably have the perfect model for non raid endgame content.

The most enjoyable thing I think I did at max level in terms of questing dates back to the BC era. I avoided daily quests like the plague for the most part, until I started wanting to ramp up my mount count for the achievements and free albino dragon and dragonhawk mounts.

There were horrifically long quest chains in Outland that drug you across multiple zones, in and out of dungeons, and sometimes even into entry-level raids to complete. Your reward was access to a higher level raid (making keys, or becoming attuned), and sometimes just a shiny trinket or item that you wouldn't see anyone else running around with because of the silly amounts of time and effort needed in order to finish the chain. This was also back when there was no LFD tool, and we had to find our own groups into dungeons by calling out for more party members in major cities. But when you ran the dungeon, and finished that bit of the quest and could carry onward in your grand adventure, it felt great for me.

The netherdrake dailies were something that I tolerated because the lead up to their daily zone was so demanding. When I was finally friendly with those blasted dragon-abusing scummy orcs, I was ecstatic. I'd infiltrated their ranks, and had access to a sort of mini-zone within the zone that others didn't. This still happens today, but it's generally part of the normal progression of quests in an area (you start off neutral with a faction, and just work your way up into more quests along the storyline of the zone), and not something extra, secretive, and out of the way that you can choose to take on for rare rewards. I still have my staff that summons the image of the Archmage, Vargoth. I'd still love to have sole access to those dungeons in the Netherstorm that I had to work so hard to get the keys for (barring those who have not unlocked the content). We did have to clear three dungeons in WotLK in order to access Icecrown, but that's hardly the venture that it was back in the day to open up a special dungeon or raid instance. Heck, we don't get to forge keys in the dying embers of the Felreaver, anymore. Without the cutscenes and all the voice acting, those quests felt epic, as opposed to having my hand held through all of the quest chains of today. I felt like a champion taking on the world out there by myself (Yes! I DID just kill that stupid gank-machine, all for this key and sweet, sweet revenge. Word.), and not a peon friend of Thrall or Malfurion who occasionally does something spiffy so they don't have to. Instead of trying to make everything accessible and everything epic (for me, watching voiced out scenes across Hyjal, Uldum, and Twilight Highlands loses the epic feeling after the first two or three in a row), maybe limit it a bit. Pull back, and let the spectacular moments shine. People might feel less disenchanted with the (admittedly) great content that you've come up with if we weren't bombarded by it's "greatness" all the time.

I enjoyed the long quest chains that gave access to new materials and content. I loved working for my attunements into raids, and scrambling around even at lvl 60, trying to get into Blackwing Lair. I don't enjoy watching story heroes chit chat, and I don't like having my hand held through every step of the way by easy-to-accomplish quests tasks and uninteresting zone development stories. Maybe that's a bit closer to what I was getting at. Right. If we focus more on the players and their accomplishments (and actually have some difficult - not grindy, not repetetive - solo content to work through), and less on the story of not-ourselves, we'd get that feeling of heroism back. Having Thrall call me a hero just doesn't cut it for me when all I did was slay a few easy-to-beat elementals. There was no struggle in reforging his soul, dont'cha know.

So, quest chains that go on forever > dailies. Unlockable content > pansy "everyone has access to everything easily" scenarios. (I know y'all mentioned that you didn't like attunements anymore, and that's a shame. I felt like I earned the right to be in those epic-hard dungeons.) And , just to throw this in, less grinding rep for rewards like common and uninteresting crafting patterns (epic, amazing patterns, sure!) and number bumping head enchants (necessary, but terribly boring as an item that I'm supposed to work a month to obtain). More working towards epic items (sure, make them heroic dungeon ilvl if you create the solo content hard enough to warrant it), mounts (you're still doing this, and that's just lovely), and generally things that are optional. That may cut down on the feeling that people need to grind these reputations and pound their fingers to bone dust with dailies. When you unlock something, it's either totally optional, or absolutely huge like a raid for raiders, or even a new PvP ground or special area.

And finally, I miss my long-winded class quests. They focused on me and my journey to getting a Dreadsteed, Charger, becoming a better Rogue, new demons, special Mage spells, totems, and Warrior stances. That was a real feeling of progression for me at the levels in which they occurred, because it was about my development as a caster/fighter/sneaky stabby person/elemental master. They are milestones that I miss dearly. No quest chain or single quest to date has outdone my Dreadsteed quest chain.
