The problem with the current crop of dailies is there is no bulk alternative to them. Say what you will about the rep grinds of old -- you had your dailies, and you had repeatable turn ins to increase your rep. If you wanted the rep but got sick of the dailies, you could substitute them for grinding mobs, or turning in items, or running dungeons or whatnot; you didn't have to do dailies. Molten Front, and to a lesser extent, Argent Tournament, were different in that you had a limited number of rep/rewards to gain per day, and that was it. If you missed a day, you were a day behind everyone else, and there was absolutely no way of catching up. That is bad design, and suggesting, "Oh, you don't have to do it," as a means of an excuse is not only silly, but disingenuous to the actual complaint being leveled.

I like dailies. But I'd like an alternative to them without running them into the ground. Netherwing had eggs and gathering profession turn ins, which was the perfect mix. More rep/daily hubs should have that, not less.

A major part of enjoyment to me is being able to collect all the patterns in the game, otherwise why be a tradesman? However, I still object to design regardless of whether I want to collect every pattern or just the important ones.

To be more specific:

My enchanter/tailorer worked non-stop to get all the patterns and went after it with gusto. I also sent cloth and greens from alts to my main without any sense of loss except not being able to DE soulbound items on non enchanter alts. I felt like I had reasonable flexibility in playing either my main or my alts without worrying about significant time lost in gaining trade patterns. I could also spend gold on the AH to buy materials needed to get patterns. I got all the patterns pretty quickly.

My blacksmith and leatherworker collect patterns in spurts, sometimes going months without working on skilling up or getting more patterns. Sometimes I spend a few hours collecting materials, crafting them up, and either skilling up my trade or using the mats to buy more patterns. I might buy ten patterns in a day and then put it aside for another month. I also have alts who can mine and they send the mats to my main blacksmith. I still have patterns to acquire but I know with a few more days of effort, I'll have them all.

In contrast, my JC has to remember to log in every day and do the daily in order to accumulate 1/3 to 1/5 of a pattern. She personally has to do the quest, I cannot hand her stacks of currency that an alt worked on, I cannot buy the currency off the AH, there is no other way to get these patterns except to log in once a day to do a daily and accumulate currency for them. If I miss a day, I extend the time it will take to get all the patterns. I don't have the option of doing these in a chunk when I decide to work on my JC - I have no choice but to log in every day and do the daily.

The Cata JC design is very inflexible and forces players to do it only one way which to me is bad design. At least in Wrath we had the broken necklace and nuking rare ore to get powder which somewhat helped speed things along though not enough for my enjoyment. I don't think we even have that in Cata. So once again I say Bad Design but at least I've (hopefully) detailed well enough why I feel it is Bad Design so at least maybe hopefully we get a change for next expansion.

Yes, it is.

Dailies aren't bad design, but the fact is progression wise for a lot of players, they DO feel somewhat mandatory in the gearing up process.

I understand but because of the way WoW is designed nowadays and the outlook of the playerbase in general, it's hard to make any new content with decent rewards without that content feeling mandatory to a lot of players, even though it may not be.
The problem is, if for example, the rep rewards from the daily faction are too weak, then yes players will skip them and not bother.
If the rep rewards however, are extremely good (even if it's just one item per class/role at Exalted), then many players are going to view that reputation and therefore those dailies, etc, as mandatory, despite that there may be other alternatives to obtaining an on-par or superior item of the same slot (raiding, valor points, etc).

Daily quest areas are actually good design though. I know some players hate gating, but it's necessary in a game like WoW.
Players who wish to grind out their MF dailies in 2 or 3 days flat for example just to get them over with, don't really want to experience content at all. They are clearly players who don't want to be doing that particular type of content, but still want the rewards from it.

One of WoW's biggest flaws in my opinion is making players feel forced to do content they don't enjoy.

If a player doesn't enjoy raiding, they shouldn't feel obliged to do it.
If a player has no interest in experience the content of daily quest areas, then similarly, they shouldn't feel obliged to and should be able to spend their time on what they do enjoy.
If a player doesn't want to run dungeons, they shouldn't feel obliged to run 7 a week to remain competitive with others on Valor point rewards.

This is one aspect I feel Vanilla and early to mid Burning Crusade were superior to Wrath/Cataclysm. Players weren't forced or obliged to do content they hated or to run content they may like so frequently that it starts to feel like a chore and then they start to hate it.


I really like that in Mists of Pandaria there will be more ways to acquire Valor Points. This is a good thing as it gives players more options to progress their character and should allow players to focus more on the types of content they enjoy.

Keep a Valor Point weekly cap, with the system designed so that most max-level players will reach that cap providing they've been relatively active that week. The whole point of Valor Points then would be to provide rewards for players progressing their character in the manner of their choosing (be it dungeons, raids, daily quests, levelling various faction reputations, levelling professions, etc etc), but keep it so the amount of VP rewarded for each thing is commensurate with player time/committment.

For example, doing all the daily quests in one area (e.g. MF dailies, Tol Barad dailies) usually takes roughly the same time it takes to do one Heroic dungeon. So for example, completing ALL the TB daily quests or ALL the MF dailies might give a total VP equal to that of one Heroic dungeon run.

Faction reps and Profession levelups could work a little differently.

Factions might give a certain amount of VP for hitting Friendly, then more for Honored, more again for Revered and a fairly sizeable amount for hitting Exalted.

Professions might give VP for every 10 levels after 525 for example, then a small VP reward every 2 levels from 585 to 600.



Naturally, dailies shouldn't be the only way to accrue rewards, and they aren't. Dailies were, in part, a response to a World of Warcraft where there wasn't a lot of incentive to come play on non-raid days, since for many players, the only way to progress became dungeon runs and, for a few, raiding. We also wanted to provide another means of acquiring currency aside from professions, and new ways to acquire reputation with important factions too. They're designed to hit a lot of notes (I'm probably missing some), and I think that they're pretty successful. You don't have to hit your cap, (indeed, one of our fears about a raised daily cap is that players might feel compelled to hit the new, higher cap) but you can if you want to put in the time. Naturally, we also want to continue to add other means of progression to the end-game, and we're looking for ways to do so in a fun and compelling way.


This is good.

Daily quest areas are good, but it would be nice if they had a strong story focus and one daily quest area progressed through to another area after you experience all the content of that area.

At the moment, the daily quest areas make you do the dailies for too long a period of time. Any content you are made to repeat over and over is going to get old. I think most people enjoy daily quest areas for about a month when they get released, but after a few weeks they get old fast.

In future, I'd like to see a larger number of daily quest areas associated with each daily rep faction and a stronger story presence and progression so we can see the repurcussions of our actions.

I'll give an example using Cataclysm:

4.0 We could have had 3 separate daily questing areas, but all associated with one faction (e.g. Servants of Deathwing). One area could have been dealing with the threat posed by Al'Akir and his Air Elementals, another dealing with Cho'gall and the Twilight's Hammer and a third dealing with Nefarian and the black dragonflight.

Each of the 3 individual areas would have their own progression (similar to Molten Front but on a much shorter timeframe - you should be able to completely unlock all the content for each daily quest area in about 2 weeks).

Each area could have had their own vendor with some non-progression based rewards (e.g. things like a tabard with a port to that area similar to the Argent Crusader's Tabard, pets, mounts, novelty items).

But the major progression rewards would all be held with the faction (Servants of Deathwing), that is associated with all 3 areas. So players would be free to earn those rewards purely by focusing on the dailies in one of those areas, two of those areas or all 3. Up to the player.
The Exalted Servants of Deathwing rewards however, could maybe require players to have unlock all content from at least 1 of the areas, e.g. as the progression from all 3 areas might then lead into the 4.2 daily areas.

Then come 4.2, we might be able to see the changes wrought to the 4.0 daily quest areas as a result of our actions and then start on the new daily quest area/s with a new faction dealing with the threat of Ragnaros. Then those quests would gradually lead us into 4.3 and another daily faction, this time dealing with Deathwing.

I get concerned when I see players throwing out words like 'bad design'. Perhaps an individual dislikes a design choice, and that's fine. We do our best, but World of Warcraft can't be all things to all people, all the time. That said, making a value judgment about whether the design is 'bad' or not is not only un-constructive, but in the vast majority of the cases I've seen, such an assessment reveals that the design was not well understood to begin with.

These forums represent an opportunity to have a dialogue about the game. I think that choosing words that have context and meaning, and offering alternative solutions, makes for feedback which is more readily useful.


I want you to understand that I agree with you about the choice of words. That's why I am saying that I disagree with your assessment of the term "bad design" as unconstructive, and I also question your choice of words in your response.

I do agree with you that the "bad design" comment reveals a lack of understanding. But really, who's to blame for that? A tiny, tiny fraction of your playerbase understands, let alone works with, the base concepts that make up decent design work. It's not an easy art! I work with software a lot, and with processes in general, and design is hands-down the hardest part of the job, and I admit that I only perform at the amateur level with it. So it falls to the designer to explain himself sufficiently that the user will understand why the choices made were made in the first place -- what problems they were designed to address, what tradeoffs were made. It's only been very recently that those of us out here are getting to hear what goes on in the minds of the designers, and I believe the dailies predated that transparency.

When you hear "bad design" that should be a signal to you that your players don't get the reasoning behind the design decisions. What it should not be seen as is an opportunity to take a superior tone and tell someone that he's made a mistake in using that term. As I said, if he doesn't understand, then it probably does look like bad design from where he sits. Your words are a very direct attempt to stifle anyone from using the term "bad design", when frankly it should fall to Blizz to explain their decisions a little more openly and clearly. You're right in that "Your design is bad" is not the same as "I don't like your design", but it's sometimes difficult to tell the difference when you're not the designer.

For the record, I would like to say that I can't imagine how you'd ever keep a game interesting enough for 11+ million people to keep coming back (and paying the monthly tithe, of course!) without something like dailies. Personally, I'd like the daily limit bumped up to around 35, but... I know you have to draw the line somewhere.
