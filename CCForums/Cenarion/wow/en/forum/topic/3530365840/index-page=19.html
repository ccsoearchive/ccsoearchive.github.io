I like the game. I didn't really like the BC version of the Molten Front (Isle of Kel'danas or whatever it was called), but I did it simply because back in BC, raids were UBER hard and the only way you could really get comparable gear that gave you a chance in the raids were through the badge gear from doing the dailies until you maxed your badge cap.

Same is true for Molten Front. The dailies are a grind, but yield the only rewards that can give you a chance in the Firelands raid.

Again, the problem is that it is the same set of dailies, over and over. First you unlock 5, then 10, then 15, the 20 and it is just mind numbingly repetitive. If they just made a super huge long quest line of 120 quests (like an entire zone has), then there wouldn't be an issue. Mount Hyjal, Vashj'ir, Deepholm, Uldum, Twilight Highlands all have their own questlines and quests. If they had just made the Molten Front its own zone, people wouldn't even balk at cranking out 125 quests.

Technically speaking, if you design something so complex that it is often misunderstood, wouldn't that actually be proof that it was poorly designed?


If that were the thought I was expressing, I might be inclined to agree. That's not what I was getting at.

It's easy for a critic to make the mistake of confusing their opinion on a system--solely in the context of their own preferred playstyle--for whether or not the system in question is well or poorly designed. Often the assessment is levied without any argument for why a given item isn't well designed, it "just is" and the rest of the argument flows from that assumption.

Merely because one dislikes something, does not necessarily mean that it is also poorly designed. It could be, certainly, but starting a discussion by stating an assumption as though it were fact isn't gauged to have an open discussion where an actual exchange of ideas takes place.
