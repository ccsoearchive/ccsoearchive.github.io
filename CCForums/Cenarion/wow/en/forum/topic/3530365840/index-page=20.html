This is just a bad design. A game should not ask for daily commitment to enjoy what it has to offer.


They ask nothing - they merely reward a choice.

We typically provide some boundaries because, as you've illustrated, something that we allow, is something that can all too easily seem mandatory. That's not what we want, but we do want to provide a reason to come into the game, be in the world, and see what's happening on a frequent basis. It's nice to also be able to offer some rewards for doing that. Naturally, dailies shouldn't be the only way to accrue rewards, and they aren't. Dailies were, in part, a response to a World of Warcraft where there wasn't a lot of incentive to come play on non-raid days, since for many players, the only way to progress became dungeon runs and, for a few, raiding. We also wanted to provide another means of acquiring currency aside from professions, and new ways to acquire reputation with important factions too. They're designed to hit a lot of notes (I'm probably missing some), and I think that they're pretty successful. You don't have to hit your cap, (indeed, one of our fears about a raised daily cap is that players might feel compelled to hit the new, higher cap) but you can if you want to put in the time. Naturally, we also want to continue to add other means of progression to the end-game, and we're looking for ways to do so in a fun and compelling way.

I get concerned when I see players throwing out words like 'bad design'. Perhaps an individual dislikes a design choice, and that's fine. We do our best, but World of Warcraft can't be all things to all people, all the time. That said, making a value judgment about whether the design is 'bad' or not is not only un-constructive, but in the vast majority of the cases I've seen, such an assessment reveals that the design was not well understood to begin with.

These forums represent an opportunity to have a dialogue about the game. I think that choosing words that have context and meaning, and offering alternative solutions, makes for feedback which is more readily useful.

I can understand that and I respect it a lot, but I do think that the design of Molten Front was absolutely horrid. Why not just make the new dailies worth more Marks of the World Tree instead of having to do every single one of them over again every single day? Why not make the next 5 dailies you unlock give you the same amount of Marks of the World Tree as you would have gotten from doing all of the previous ones (per day) plus the new 5? Having to grind all of those dailies in that laggy zone over and over and over and over and over and over and over was terrible. Solution:

Zone 1 = 5 daily quests. Those quests give (for example) 5 tokens each. When you have 250 tokens, the next zone unlocks.

Zone 2 = 5 daily quests. Those quests give 10 tokens each. When you have 500 tokens, the next zone unlocks.

Zone 3 = 3 daily quests. Those quests give 20 tokens each. When you have....the next zone unlocks.

That way we're not having to do every quest from zone 1, every quest from zone 2, and every quest from zone 3 every single day. You can do a few and be done with it and go back about your business.

Blizzard cant win in this situation.

Players demand more non-raid, solo friendly content. Blizzard delivers with daily quest chains like firelands. Then players get mad because blizzard has to gate the content so people dont plow through it in a day and whine for more. Blizzard cant buff up the difficulty because the average player for these has the skills of a blind gopher and would simply complain they are too hard. Since they made them easy for solo purposes, blizzard has to gate them so they last. Players complain about gating instead.

Blizzard has two options to have solo content be meaningful: give it a grind or make it more appropriately difficult to match the rewards. Thats basic game design 101. The problem is that the skill baseline for a massive chunk of players is so low (yay spirit hunters) that the devs felt that gating was the only solution. Not only that, but due to the high consumption rate of solo content, blizzard would have to be churning out new stuff at a crazy high rate because the shelf-life for such content is a fraction of group content.

No real answers sadly.
