I posted about this topic a long time ago: http://us.battle.net/wow/en/forum/topic/2661297139?page=2#23

I feel compelled to state that most things in WoW are choice, not requirements. You don't have to Raid, or do Dungeons, or PvP, or do Dailies. You can if you want to, but odds are there will be something you like doing and Blizzard has done a commendable job of trying to offer something for everything. Could they do better? Perhaps, but that isn't the problem.

The grind is. The RNG is. Doing something over, and over, and over again, not because the content is "fun", but because the reward is "worth" slogging through the same thing you done a million times over until you've unlocked or looted what you really wanted.

Case in point: Holiday Mounts. I preferred the daily quest approach the first Brewfest had. I knew that as long as I participated in the holiday by doing the events and quests and running the boss fights, that I would unlock the mounts and be happy. This year however I've just decided to give up. Four years of doing the Headless Horseman every single day and no luck on the drop. If it weren't for my Paladin doing the dailies the first year, the only character I would have a Brewfest mount on would be my Priest, who got it last year.

Now Blizzard does these things to "keep them rare and special". I get that, but I don't like it. I'm not the kind of person who obsesses over what other people have that I do not. I only care about what rewards I can get in the time that I have to spend getting them. I could care less if someone else has a Horseman mount or Firelands bird, but I do care about my ability to get those things if I so desire them.

But, as I mentioned in my linked post, Blizzard is caught between the rock and hard place MMO design flaw: you simply cannot make content fast enough and diverse enough to please everyone. Your solutions to solve grinds will in turn become grinds. Players who see great rewards will always feel compelled to go after them and they will nearly always choose the easiest and straightest path there....even if they hate every second of it. The developers simply can't win in this system, so they gate the content to make it last longer and they gate the rewards to keep the carrot on a stick dangling so those who feel slighted by the gating don't give up too quickly.

The is no magic pill solution either. People have been discussing these problems for years and the best solution is still "weeklies". That should tell us all something about how difficult it is to think outside of the box in game design.

They ask nothing - they merely reward a choice.

We typically provide some boundaries because, as you've illustrated, something that we allow, is something that can all too easily seem mandatory. That's not what we want, but we do want to provide a reason to come into the game, be in the world, and see what's happening on a frequent basis. It's nice to also be able to offer some rewards for doing that. Naturally, dailies shouldn't be the only way to accrue rewards, and they aren't. Dailies were, in part, a response to a World of Warcraft where there wasn't a lot of incentive to come play on non-raid days, since for many players, the only way to progress became dungeon runs and, for a few, raiding. We also wanted to provide another means of acquiring currency aside from professions, and new ways to acquire reputation with important factions too. They're designed to hit a lot of notes (I'm probably missing some), and I think that they're pretty successful. You don't have to hit your cap, (indeed, one of our fears about a raised daily cap is that players might feel compelled to hit the new, higher cap) but you can if you want to put in the time. Naturally, we also want to continue to add other means of progression to the end-game, and we're looking for ways to do so in a fun and compelling way.

I get concerned when I see players throwing out words like 'bad design'. Perhaps an individual dislikes a design choice, and that's fine. We do our best, but World of Warcraft can't be all things to all people, all the time. That said, making a value judgment about whether the design is 'bad' or not is not only un-constructive, but in the vast majority of the cases I've seen, such an assessment reveals that the design was not well understood to begin with.

These forums represent an opportunity to have a dialogue about the game. I think that choosing words that have context and meaning, and offering alternative solutions, makes for feedback which is more readily useful.


It's perfectly reasonable for someone to put forward an argument that such and such mechanic is "bad design" -- as long as they back it up with an explanation about why it's bad. If they fail to provide a compelling case, then yes it just falls into the "I don't like this" bucket.

However, it's not reasonable to deride a good argument just because you feel the term is overused.

There are plenty of examples of bad design in WoW, just as there are plenty of examples of good design. It's a big game. No need to get all huffy about it.

I've always enjoyed dailies for the most part... there are two factors that would lead me to file complaint with them. I wouldn't call these factors "bad design" as regards the daily system in general, but I would call them poor implementation.

1) The limit of 25/day. The only reason I have ever seen given is so that players don't feel compelled to do more than that... yet, for newer characters, this simply means they have to spend months longer to accomplish an equal amount. You're not cutting down on the total time investment, only on how much of it can be done in a given day. Given that each quest, by definition, can be done only once per day, further limiting the ability of the players to do what they want seems.... at best, counterproductive. At worst, it seems like a blatant play for money (if you want to do these things it'll take you at least four months, better stay subscribed!)

2) The fact that to collect everything from some dailies takes *far* too long. I understand that when the expansion was new, you wanted to limit the availability of jewelcrafting patterns... but what about a new player that hits level 85 now? There are plenty of players with the patterns, so the cost of patterns no longer serves to limit availability, but the new player still has to spend months collecting tokens to have a good collection of patterns.


In short, I like the daily system... but the mere fact that these quests can only be done once per is a limitation. Further limitations make the system feel restrictive. In the past, you have said that forcing players to do only 25 dailies in a day offers them a choice of what to do. My counter has always been "Being forced to choose one of two in-game activities that I want to do isn't a fun choice, it's a restriction, and one I resent". Furthermore, it increases the gap between old and new players. When the molten front went live, achievement collectors on old characters could happily do the new dailies, because they had all the old stuff already. Someone who hit level 85 a week after the molten front went live? Well, they had to choose between Netherwing, Ogri'La, Skettis, Argent Tournament, Molten Front, Twilight Highlands, Tol Barad... in short, they were punished for being new and wanting to do everything the game has to offer.
