I have a question then: If the intent is to give players a choice, why is it that the Molten Front dailies award gear upgrades from tier 11? Unless you raided firelands, those dailies weren't a choice, they were a path to progression (which, in my opinion, was a wrong decision to make).

Daily quests like the Netherwing ones in Outland or the Argent Tournament in Northrend feel like a "choice" because ultimately the rewards involved have no bearing on your character's power (mounts and pets are cool to have, but aren't by any means mandatory). They feel optional. For non-rading characters, the Molten Front (and to a certain extent, Tol Barad) dailies don't "feel" optional at all, because opting out of them means you're giving up gear upgrades, which is never a cool thing to do.

Any comments on that?


There are plenty of other dailies where the rewards have been non-power related. The goal was different for Molten Front, and we deliberately wanted to offer a separate path to power for interested players. So, we wanted it to be compelling in that way. Still, it remains that there are alternative, non-raid, paths to power aside from Molten Front.


I wanted to pick this quote for a reason. Because it illustrates my problems with the Molten Front and the good things about the MF in the same place. As the poster stated, the Argent Tourney and Netherwing daily quest hubs gave us an opportunity to complete something that did not effect our player power, but effected certain cosmetic and fun aspects of the game. This was a tremendous addition to the game as a whole. This was content "options" at the end game. (I'd also toss in the Skywing/Ogrila daily hubs into this "not needed for player power, but gave us gameplay options" category as well.)

However, Sunwell Isle and Molten Front dailies were created for an entirely different purpose. Do I think that either one was implemented perfectly? Hell no. Which model would I like to see in the future? Sunwell of course. The Sunwell dailies gave every one the same profession options based on rep and opened up options for players who were dungeoneers and raiders to gain power as the Molten Front dailies gave through sheer personal grind. The Sunwell model was more interesting and more rewarding across the server. Most importantly, a player did not have to participate in 30 days of dailies on each of their characters once things were unlocked for one.

In the end, there is a real need for "options" and daily quest hubs are a huge part of making these options available to the masses. However, we need more "options" period. We currently, have 4 options for end game play. (Notice I do not say advancement, because not everything has to be about advancement, ie Argent Tournament.) 1) Raiding, 2) Dungeons, 3) Dailies, 4) Gathering/Crafting/Working the AH. That's really it. You could say, "leveling alts" but that is not "end-game play". We need more options.

The single reason I am still subscribing was the mention of PvE Scenarios in MoP. I certainly hope they do not go the way of Path of the Titans (although I am afraid they may.) Even if these scenarios were pushed into a PvE "Arena" type category (and I hope of scenarios work, we will see a PvE arena in the future.) Where we get random monsters in waves that we must take down with a full group of 5. We just desperately need other ways to spend out time in game and feel like we are improving.

Oh and for the love of god, stop giving Valor Points to everything. If you want multiple options for players to advance, give multiple currencies from multiple methods. Give DMF tickets, Valor Points, Scenario Cash, Pet Battle Bucks, Daily Quest Tokens, all of which could be traded in for differing and separate gear. Take some time, not everything has to be perfect, not everything has to be the same, but we are sick and tired of getting Valor from multiple sources, and we are tired of everything being in the same vendor.

Now getting a bit off topic, I think the root of all these problems is blizzards idea that "Everyone needs to be able to experience ALL the content". It really set this game in the wrong direction.

In classic and some of BC, being a "hardcore" player wasn't nearly all the e-peen. It was about a feeling of epicness, a sort of epicness that comes from doing content very few people experience (thus making the content itself "special") It wasn't exclusive to 20 hour a day 40 man raiders, because the game gave other ways to go about this as well, because the game was so fresh, whether making Pvp vids, or simply running to the highest places in Azeroth haha. All that kind of stuff. And it gave casuals something a LOT more meaningful to play towards, actually seeing more of the game rather then some purple text that isn't hard to get or relevant at the least.

And it doesn't need to be literal content, as in assets. It could be a specific AV match that was really epic, or world pvp, or whatever, epicness generated by the players themselves.

When your showing everyone the content upfront in the interests of casuals, you're removing almost all motivation from raiding or doing any other content besides challenge and e-peen. This causes a lot of problems from all sides of the playerbase. You're hard pressed to keep a lot of raiders in the game as long term subscribers simply because a large amount of them actually don't care about the e-peen of downing heroics or w/e. You're also screwing over a lot of casual players, because they can get bored of content too, especially when all of it is so accessible, and they slowly lose motivation to get gear realizing they can never actually get anything meaningful. And challenge wise, WoW simply isn't a skillful game, your own RTS surpass its pvp and raid difficulty is even more of a joke for the individual player, its just moores law aggravated by the occasional scrub that makes it hard.

Thats part of the reason why dailies are so useless right now. They're almost universally grindy, but serve no purpose within the current motivating drives of the game. semi-casuals know it won't ever make there 7/7 normal into a 7/7 heroic, and its even worse on the hardcore. It isn't just the grind, its also the neurotic context in which the grind occurs.

Something blizzard forgets is nobody (sane) really cares about purples intrinsically, they care because something blizzard did right made them care about the world itself, that is a per-requisite for in game rewards being meaningful.
