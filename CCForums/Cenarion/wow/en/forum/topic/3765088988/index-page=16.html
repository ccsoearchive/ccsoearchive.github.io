Once upon a time, the only people who could do certain content and get certain gear were those who formed large guilds and organized everyone being on at the same time. The people who would go on these raids only had to log in at a specified time once a week and play for a couple hours, but nonetheless considered themselves hardcore, and superior to someone who played every day for several hours and had common gear.

The entire casual/hardcore argument has always been flawed that way, simply because there has never been a correlation between time and effort put into the game and the gear you get. Those with exclusive gear certainly want to think they're more hardcore than everyone else, because hey, check out my rare gear, but they haven't really put more effort or time into the game than everyone else. Now that commoners can queue up for raids as well, it completely shatters the illusion of superiority for them, because just being able to attempt raids was the only thing they had over everyone else. It was never about time or effort, or being 'hardcore'.


While this sounds good (and back in BC, it's how I felt), it's just not true. The difference between a hardcore raider and a casual is research. The hardcore raider studies a fight before he/she ever goes into it. They know when to use cooldowns and get out of fire before it lands, not after they're half-dead because they know when it's coming and what to look for.

The RF is the perfect tool for casuals to not only see endgame content and but feel involved with the game. The problem is comments like "You're bad" when someone dies. That's hurtful and immature. They know they're bad, does it make you feel good about yourself to tell them? Mixing experienced players with inexperienced players is what makes LFR work, but when the experienced players are mean-spirited, it's not fun for anyone. I don't know how to fix arrogance, so maybe we just have to take the bad with the good.

It's like the kick feature in Dungeons now, that's both good and bad too. I never worry about a player who can't dps well. I figure I can help carry him. But then someone starts saying "You're bad" and initiates a kick. Before you know it, that guys gone. I feel bad for them. It's not their fault, they're just trying to learn.

You see, I've pretty much given up raiding. I'm probably going to look to go back to it full time in MoP, but right now, I have bigger things to worry about. I'm exactly what you'd call a casual raider, between alts I'll get into whatever pug I can, it's painfully unlikely my guild is ever going to need to use a dps backup, but im there if I'm needed...

That said, I understand these fights, not just what I read on some strat that tells you where to stand, but WHY you do it that way. I know the priority of what slime you kill depends more on your raid group and where it's strengths and weaknesses are than on reading that one color > another.

I know the reason you split your dps to bounce the orb around is so that there's ALWAYS enough people to split the damage between to make healing as easy as possible, and avoid people just dying (which is why you shouldn't split on LFR, because a single person can handle the orb which has a 100% predictable spawn point). Chaining the lightning on Hagara should only take a few seconds, you don't need 25 people running as a group in the same circle, your ranged should be back far enough that you chain to the east and west one almost instantly after the add dies, while ranged should be able to get it to the South one in only a few seconds from that point, leaving melee at north, ready to charge back into the boss, cutting down on healing needed A LOT.

Amalgamation SHOULD be low in HP by the time you get your 9 stacks, but since forced AoE and tanks and everything factors into it, you should usually stop around 30% in LFR to make up for it being so squishy, that AoE adds pointless healing needed, and BACK UP when its on 9 stacks. And if all your ranged is in melee range, the bloods only have one place to go, so tanks don't have to run around trying to get bloods, there's no AoE before that 9th stack, ranged can stack on melee. You can hit the tendon from the middle too.

Or the 4th platform on madness, after you kill the tentacle, you should only do SLOW dps until hemorrhage, because blood + tentacle = really really bad. To be safe, you can even actually stop DPS until after you kill the bloods, it's not the huge DPS race people make it out to be.

But to me, it's all common sense. What works for one group, doesn't necessarily work for all of them. You can read a strat, you can watch videos, it only takes 5 minutes, but it's all useless unless you understand WHY you do it. The most casual of players can figure this stuff out, it really just comes down to how well you can pay attention.

To me, casual vs hardcore is in the commitment you make, not how good you are, or how much effort you put into knowing what you're supposed to do. To me, I'm casual because I play when I want to.
