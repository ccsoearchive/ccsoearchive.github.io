I think you've misread the thrust of my argument.

The dungeons in cata were retuned upwards compared to those of Wrath. There was no beginning-style raid such as Kara or Naxx to ease new and casual players looking to step up their game into raiding. In addition a lot of player mechanics, especially healing mechanics were drastically changed. This made running dungeons and raids in the initial phases of the expansion a lot less compelling for casual players and those serious players with limited play times.

In addition, Cataclym's storylines were disjointed compared with Wrath's. There was a lot more player-interaction with Arthas in Wrath than there was with Deathwing in Cata, for example. The story was thus less compelling for casual players that mostly played for the questing and story. In addition, there was less content with only five levels available for end-game players as well. Unfortunately, given the demands of the redesign of 1-60 this was probably going to happen no matter what.

Blizzard basically alienated a substantial number of players with this divergent approach. Not enough content on one hand and overly tuned content on the other.

Adding different levels of difficulty for content (such as the LFR) helps to solve the problems caused by this approach. It opens up more content for different levels of players. If the LFR is not challenging enough, people can move up to Normals.

As to whether Blizzard listened to a "vocal minority" or Bob from accounting, the fact of the matter is they retuned difficulty and mechanics upwards for a major portion of the game content and lost subscriptions from the very beginning of the expansion. You and I may both agree we want challenging content. We should have challenging content. But I disagree with the OP that they should only cater to one portion of the player base and exclude everyone else from it. Having different levels of difficulty is not a game design sin. Providing content that only serves one part of the player base means players will go elsewhere.

“It became clear that it wasn’t realistic to try to get the audience back to being more hard core, as it had been in the past.”

I think this thread is relevant: http://us.battle.net/wow/en/forum/topic/3765088846

I would have linked a different thread as well that outlined everything that would be necessary to get rid of JP/VP, but it was deleted.

Meh, well here it is other than the introduction that noted the success of SW:ToR.


People have often discussed the state of raiding in previous expansions, so it's pretty well understood why the game has taken the course it has. Vanilla and TBC did not have easy modes and didn't have free gear, so gaining an opportunity to see the most recently released content was a lengthy and extensive process for any new player or alt of an experienced player. This lead to siphoning of geared players by the top guilds on a server which, especially in TBC with the jump from 10 to 25 players in raids beyond Karazhan, discouraged casual raiders and prevented them from progressing in content. Top guilds that didn't wish to antagonize other guilds on the server were forced to continuously raid old content to gear up replacements and alts for the latest raids, especially for key roles like the tank.

The important point here is that casual guilds did not naturally progress to more difficult content, but were stuck in a 'vicious cycle' of gearing up new members only to have them move up the 'prestige ladder' to more progressed guilds. The token system for gear, where Blizzard releases item vendors with gear equivalent to the previous raid tier with every major patch, was seen as necessary because of this specific situation.

However, as a consequence most players don't feel older content is worth doing as an organized group on a schedule, because it gives gear that's no better than what can be obtained from 5-person dungeons. Since Wrath many people have had the ability to do old raid content solo or in a group, so it's not like raids aren't interesting but clearly they are not fun enough to do in a group without a gear reward. There are exceptions and some servers do Transmogrification runs since it was announced, but many people who say they enjoyed LFR and seeing Deathwing also said that they have not had the opportunity to do any previous raid content, thus pointing out a huge deficiency in WoW's current system that has not be solved by the token system of Justice/Valor points for high-quality gear nor even the gear resets that happen every expansion to make previous content trivial for a full raid group.


Now SW:ToR is finally presenting what may be a real challenge to Blizzard's subscription base, there should be no excuse not to explore all possible options to make the game more appealing for both casual and hardcore players. Wrath showed conclusively that "easy" is NOT enough to keep players in the game, but Cataclysm taught Blizzard as well that 'challenge' comes second to accessibility for keeping players in the game. This brings us to a possible opportunity to explore why casual raid guilds in TBC, and even vanilla, could not progress to more difficult content and how to fix it, as well as why some players have not felt they could participate in even the 10-person raiding that has continued as an option from TBC until now.

There are two main reasons:
  • It wasn't fun.
  • It wasn't accessible to the casual guild.


These are both explained below, but there is also a counterbalancing factor for why Blizzard has delayed action and avoided an analysis of these problems while WoW's subs continued to drop: Blizzard wants to uphold as far as possible the Everquest-era idea that "the order in which PvE bosses are defeated after being introduced indicates the skill level of players in a guild". According to this, even if a particular player has to take a break from the game for whatever reason, simply belonging to a guild like Death and Taxes is seen as a mark of distinction due to guilds imposing a selection criteria which is equal in discriminating ability to the actual game encounters themselves.

It must be recognized that making raiding more fun and accessible to casual players will decrease the correlation between PvE progression in the most recently released content and the skill of players and therefore runs counter to the EQ legacy philosophy of the purpose of raiding. However, one of the major reasons this would happen is that skilled players would see less reason to join the existing 'top' guilds. The current system is based on the assumption that hardcore players have a higher priority on PvE progression than on their social group or server community in order to derive any meaningful correlation between PvE progress and individual player skill at all.


This tradeoff between the flawed, but undeniable use of guild PvE progression to indicate player skill and the fun of the game for casual players must be recognized, but eliminating the VP/JP system would open the opportunity for alternate ways of indicating skill similar to the feel of vanilla in the scarcity of higher qualities of gear. Accomplishing this in PvE and PvP without upsetting casual players is the challenge faced by Blizzard, but everything indicates that it should be a reasonable goal if Blizzard doesn't want to lose to SW:ToR.

One barrier is the loot system discouraging casual players who want to be "nice" and drama free, see this thread:
http://us.battle.net/wow/en/forum/topic/3754955563

Note that while it would make the game more fun by reducing conflict, it would also decrease the accuracy of PvE completion order to measure skill since any hardcore guilds that used it would not be 100% optimizing for overall raid performance.

Another barrier is the type of challenges offered in PvE, and whether single players regularly have the responsibility of preventing the raid from wiping. While "give player A a 10% chance to wipe the raid by failing at raid mechanic X" has the same measuring capacity as "give player A a 30% chance to fail mechanic X, but the raid only wipes if players B and C can't recover from it which happens 30% of the time", the second situation is more fun to many casual players because of distributed responsibility and less possibility of finger-pointing. Making threat difficult again, to the point where tanks must gear to hold aggro and still expect to lose it often, would be one example of how to change the type of challenge offered in PvE and the resulting chain, or trajectory, of possibilities after a mistake (or a disconnect).

Another barrier is the evaluation of players who don't have the highest possible character progression when joining a group of strangers in PvE. If raid PvE is not inherently fun and rewarding then raiders will naturally be upset when a poorly-geared player joins their dungeon group since it decreases the chances of success; if raiders aren't required to run 5-person dungeons (by removing VP/JP for randoms) or the speed of PvE raid completion is no longer seen as a significant indicator of individual skill, then group PvE will be more fun for casual players.

Another barrier is whether old raid content is still "relevant" and challenging. Blizzard has tentatively announced several possible changes in MoP that could cause this to happen.

Another barrier is finding other players willing to participate in group content. LFR has potential for finding one-time PUGers for this, depending on several factors on how players expect to use it, but it does not help a 5-person guild of friends find other guilds on the same server for running 25-person raid content (old or recent) and additional methods might be useful. Note that a culture of guild alliances for completing raid content is another reason for a decrease in accuracy of content completion order to measure skill.

Another barrier is attitudes in PvP towards losing to players with better gear. By utilizing a rank system Blizzard can change what it means to "win" in PvP and make PvP more enjoyable for all players who enjoy challenges and unpredictable environments.

Another barrier is the in-game justifications given for PvE content. Much of what's missing is simply any semblance of challenge for older encounters or the easier raid difficulty settings, but not entirely because raids vary hugely in popularity even if everyone has had access to it. Trial of the Crusader received a very low popularity rating in official Blizzard polls (EU), while other raid instances are more popular. Mop's instanced Scenarios might provide a similar function but maybe without a need to farm for gear.


SUMMARY: If the game mechanics make content more accessible to casual players even at higher difficulties, then 'free' epics (minimal skill required and large proportion of the playerbase in possession), teleporting to all instances, rewards for random dungeon/battleground that create tension between people doing quests and those who only want to complete the instance, and the conflict between casual and hardcore players can all go away and in return have mechanics that would support fun and optional (on PvE servers) world PvP.
