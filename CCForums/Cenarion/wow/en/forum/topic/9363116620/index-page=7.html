She had an hour or so while the children napped.

It wasn't meant to be like this, of course. She had come here as a diplomat and had been treated with utmost decorum by the nearest village: a suite at the mayor's mansion, silk gowns, strands of jade beads and pearls, and an entourage of able bodies to keep her from harm and perhaps even actual work.

Yet, a fortnight into the venture, the archivist found herself acting as a matron for the makeshift orphanage. In lieu of silks, jewels, and immaculate coiffures, she wore sensible cotton gowns, sturdy boots, and her hair in a simple braid down her back. She looked even younger as she mothered a houseful of children.

Construction was still underway, so she had no room of her own in their temporary quarters, only a cot amidst the children's; her office was the neat stacks of papers and stationary arranged under her bed. She sat on her cot now, leaning against the wall, going through some papers. The archivist started and set down what she'd been reading as a boy climbed into her lap.

"Sister Dawnsong?" whispered the toddler, blinking sleepily, "Why do you look so sad?" He reached up to cup his tiny paws around Benoite's cheeks. The child was rewarded with a brilliant smile and the sweet lull of her voice.

"I'm not sad at all, Bo. Quite happy. I'm reading letters from..."

But that word would not come. Home.

"...From friends. Back in the Eastern Kingdoms. Here, you must try one of Miss Fizzlesprocket's biscuits." Before the child could ask any more questions, Benoite dangled a fresh biscuit before Bo's nose. The boy nearly bit Benoite's fingertips in his eagerness.

While Bo munched on the biscuit, the archivist returned to sorting through letters and packages. Some she couldn't bear. Not just yet. A glossy postcard from the Darkmoon Faire distracted her. As she turned the card over, Benoite beamed to see the sprawling cursive of Charmian Sarasri Haleshorn.

Hi Archivist Dawnsong!

I was bummed that you didn't come visit us last Sunday with Dr. Emberbane, but she told us how positively swamped you are in the boonies of Pandaria.

The Faire has come around again, and I know how you hate to miss it, so Nightsworn and I pooled our allowances to splurge on tokens. We're doing positively EVERYTHING this year.

I'm SO nervous about leading a salon but I wanted to write you to thank you for the opportunity. It means a lot that you and Dr. Emberbane recommended me for the job.

Everyone misses you loads. Nightsworn says hello!

Charm x


"Don't be sad, Sister Dawnsong!" Bo smooshed his paws on the corners of Benoite's lips and pushed them upwards. "Smile!"

Benoite gently swatted the boy's paws away and opened the biscuit tin again. "One more biscuit then back to bed with you, Bo."

"Yes, Sister Dawnsong."

The war machine sat in his room, toiling slowly over a cauldron of madness.

It had taken months to complete, his room. Archivist Dawnsong's specifications were extra-ordinary, requiring the master craft of artificers, carpenters, and a very well-paid magician. She scythed into Liore's personal coffers, facilitating perfection.

The room was spacious, open. A hard arc-shape, sprawled imperiously over the second floor of Gideon's Cathedral, above the common wing. Clean and modern, pale white and silver walls adorned by sweeping curtains and sheets of black and violet. It was like being enclosed by the sails of a fleet, barricaded against the tide of the world beyond.

Hanging linens concealed massive glass double-doors, leading to a balcony which overlooked the Dawnsong Gardens. The wall adjacent was hewn from marble and granite, with a near-mute waterfall bubbling gently down its stone surface. Illuminated by in-wall lighting, to cast a tranquil golden glow room-long, to trace ghostly patterns across the dark carpets and the sheeted walls.

The ceiling was high, invisible. She'd gathered thousands of tiny motes of light and cast them high into the air, where they would hang and mirror the constellations. Thick clouds of inky night swirled like black mist high amongst them; as the evening drew long, he could peer at his ceiling as if at the twinkling sky, drifting along with the slow ease of the ages.

A spartan bed brooded in the far corner, its taut linens untouched. A reclining, white leather sofa had been arranged beside it, now dragged before the waterwall. A single stand supported a tiny table with a tiny bowl. A sha-infested goldfish mouthed at the single strand of sea grass plopped indifferently in its center. Its many eyes swivelled and searched with demoralized apathy.

His room would be a place of repose. No distractions.

Inquisitor Bloodwing was seated on his sofa with steeped fingers, wearing his ring, his tattoo, and a sock. The harsh points of his acid stare shimmered back at him from the rolling ripples of the waterwall.

“Your pardons, Inquisitor...”

“Distinguished.”

A hesitation at the door. A hand smoothing skirts. “Your pardons, distinguished Inquisitor. I have your letters.”

Liore Bloodwing shifted on his sofa, languidly crossing one leg over the other. He did not look at the door. He affected a straighter posture, more chaste. He offered no reply; the slender figure in the doorway continued in a demure whisper.

“The weekend's salon will present a discussion of King Lich.”

Wilmur Shakyspear. Harsh points of poison flickered down to the carpet, where a first edition of Vennice and Adonic had been cast against the wall. It was face down, pages ruined.

“Yes. Good. Thank you,” Liore growled. He put a great deal of effort into sound surly, but the figure by the doorway knew better. There was a private smile, veiled behind a lofted hand.

“If you are needing nothing else, distinguished Inquisitor...”

“No. I am fine.”

The skirted vision swept into a low curtsey, before retreating. A tattered voice from near the waterwall halted it mid-heel click.

“Curly.”

“Yes boss.”

“Your wig.”

Curly frowned and reached up to adjust the dark curls, which had drifted askew during the exchange. He licked his lips with a cheesy grin and affected another curtsy. A soldier's fingers smoothed his borrowed skirts and dead man's cackle whisked him out the door.

Bloodwing stared intently at the rippling marble wall, turning his silver ring over and over and over.
