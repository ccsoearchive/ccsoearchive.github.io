[ UPDATED: For more information on The Royal Library, please visit http://theroyallibraryguild.wordpress.com ]

Boasting the largest collection of books, art, and artifacts in the Eastern Kingdoms,
The Royal Library is a joint venture between Silvermoon and Undercity to document and preserve the rich history of Azeroth.

Sponsored by a generous grant from an anonymous benefactor, the Library will be hosting a series of salons to share the treasures from its archives with the general public. Our goal is to promote discussion around these precious relics and to foster a better understanding of our shared culture.

The salons will take place every Sunday at 5pm, but location will vary. Please check the Library notices regularly, or join our mailing list to receive personalized invitations.

To Miss Benoite Dawnsong,
Archivist, Royal Library,
Silvermoon

From Greatmother Arjah,
Homeland,
The Barrens

My Dear Miss Dawnsong:

Congratulations on your salon in Sen'jin Village this Sunday last. I had not expected such an august gathering of Silvermoon's social elite -- though perhaps, when the Royal Library hosts, such things are taken as granted.

Suffice it to say, it was quite the most illustrious collection of elves that I believe Sen'jin has seen. I hope our customs did not dismay your guests too greatly. The Darkspear are a simple people, but an ancient one, and much of that simplicity is born from ten thousand years of trial and error. Like a diamond, we have become bright and hard, and perhaps a little sharp, under enormous pressure.

But you of course have no need of me to tell you these things, as you already have a cultivated student of the Darkspear and their ancestors on your staff. Convey my compliments to Master Hunter Zukkin (as you style him) -- on his dancing as well as his research.

Regarding your offer of a similar exhibition of my own work, I could perhaps be tempted, provided that it were informal and if possible shared with other readers, so that I did not seem too much the center of attention. I had quite enough of that in my younger days, and I know that participants in such things would always rather be speaking than listening.

If your schedule allows, we should take our leisure in some pleasant place and speak of the arrangements over tea.

In all fondness,

Arjah

Tonight, the weight of the Library’s seal felt heavy on her hand. The board meeting had not gone well and she was coming home to a nearly empty cathedral.

He had left for her, upon Gideon’s throne, a first edition of Greatmother Arjah’s collected sonnets and a letter from the poet herself. And because he was gone--to his bloody battles and savage wars--she allowed herself to sit in his rightful place and sip on the vintage of comfort called solace.

She took up so little of that dark throne, but she tried to mold herself as she had seen him on that first night, legs dangling over an armrest. The elaborately carved Tristfal oak was unforgiving, any which way she turned.

How had he ever slept here.

She closed her eyes but could withstand only minutes of the torture before shifting. At last she found a pose that was not so unbearable. Then, she opened the Greatmother’s letter, reading it several times. It was surreal to be so commended by a literary idol--but the Archivist’s shame was too great to savor the praise fully.

Sensationalist drivel, the board had called the Greatmother’s latest work. ‘Blackhand the Destroyer’ would get no publicity through the Library, whether or not the likes of a single Archivist had secured private funding for her plebeian events.

The next salon would be no salon at all. A fundraiser, the board had decreed. A wine tasting. Not another troll thing, for goodness sake. We heard that there weren’t even seats for your guests last time. Is this true?

Biting her bottom lip, Benoite set the Greatmother’s letter aside and curled her hands around the edges of the book of sonnets resting in her lap.

There had been a time when hands before hers might have thumbed its pages with tenderness, before the parchment was so yellowed. She opened the faded cover, silken with age. On a corner of the title-page, in script so achingly familiar to her--To A, Yours--

She closed the book swiftly.

Wincing--from the discomfort of her seat, surely--Benoite sat up and looked around to ensure that none of the Snipes had witnessed her disarray. This wouldn’t do. She had a fundraiser to organize.
