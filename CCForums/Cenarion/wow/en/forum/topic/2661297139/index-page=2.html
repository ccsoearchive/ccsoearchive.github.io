It is sad that, after so many years of MMO's, the grind is still a major design feature.

Some people like this. That's fine. It should NOT be the major play style any more.

Really, aren't there any new ideas out there? Games don't have a feeling of exploration any more, no puzzle-solving game play, no fear of the unknown.

Add on to it the fact that end-game hasn't changed at all and you can easily see how badly the "next new thing" is needed.

Lately, all MMO's are the same. Grind out levels, grind out gear, grind out professions, grind out gold. Different graphics, slightly different interface/class/race, same old grind.

Is it any wonder that their are gamers out there that jump from title to title endlessly? They aren't looking for new, they are looking for DIFFERENT.

I Beta test as many games as I can get into because it is a cheaper way of looking for "different" without breaking the bank. Same goes for Free Trials. The more, the better as we constantly search for the Holy Grail of MMO's.

Some people hold out hope that Blizzard's "Titan" will be that Holy Grail, but in all honesty if they were going to change the way an MMO plays you'd think they'd do it with a WoW expansion first to see if it works.

Certainly at some level of time/reward they would lose people because the bulk of players could do everything there is to do faster than they can come out with a new patch. But we are nowhere near that level for anyone except people with no lives who play the game 24/7.


It isn't about speed imo, but effort versus reward. Do people enjoy a challenging raid or dungeon? Absolutely! But after you've learned how the content works, the challenge is less about mechanics and teamwork and more about endurance. How many more runs will it take before the item I want drops? How many more runs will it take before I have enough points/rep to get ______? When you start measuring game play like that, things start feeling less like fun and more like grinding.

PvP is no different, btw. "Honor grind" has been a common term since Vanilla.

The funny thing is that Blizzards solutions, or attempts, are just as grindy. When players desired less random drops from bosses, Blizzard introduced the badge system. Great, a way for players to at least get something eventually just by playing! In itself though, it was also a grind. Badges turned into points, but you're still grinding out the same instances over and over to get what you want.

When they changed the honor system so that you could spend points on honor gear, they raised the prices of everything to keep players from buying everything in a week. 30k honor just for a weapon? Yeesh. Capping hasn't made any difference either....you just have more incentive to spend it faster instead of hording it.

Dailies? What started as a way to make quick gold at the level cap with once a day quests became yet another grind. Isle, Tournament, and soon to be Firelands....not to mention every holiday event has dailies, some of the tradeskills do, and of course various rep factions have dailies too (Hodir, Therazane for example).

Blizzard needs to figure out how to offer dynamic content that rewards players because they participated, while hiding the grind behind...gasp....fun. I'm not sure that's possible under the current MMO design, although games like Rift do try.

I don't think it helps that, like difficulty, they tend to fail at making a curve/spread. And the artificial caps just make it worse.

Lets say you're a dungeoneer who is looking at jp/vp items. (although raiding will be in the same place next patch =P)

jp items are a day-week's grind away (for the full set, depending on how much you want to play) that you can do as often as you want, and control the speed of the grind as you wish.

vp has a (arbitrary, since you can still do dungeons) cap, where you can only do so many/week, and if you want to keep up (which yes, in the dungeoneer example isn't a huge worry) you -have- to do it in the same amount every week. (And yes, every day was just as obnoxious).

So I don't think it's so much that you have to grind something out, I just think the arbitrary limits put on it make the "grind" look so much worse than it would be without them.

Rested experience was put in to help alleviate this type of feeling.
The 7 dungeons/week instead of 1/day was too.
But didn't help much, I don't think 1 week is long enough to help that feeling.

I feel, thinking about it, if they want to slow the rate you can do something, an accumulative total would work much better than x amount/y days.
So (for the dungeoneering example) allow people to do 1 heroic/day. But allow it to accumulate. Start counting the days from a patch or when they hit 85 (or even from a certain achievement or something).

tldr:

I don't think it's so much that you have to grind for something, it's that if you're not doing the artificially limited grind a certain amount within a specified timeframe, you're falling behind with no way to catch up that makes it feel so much worse than a real grind.

It applies to pretty much every aspect of end game play:

- The amount of dungeons/raids you have to run to buy a piece of VP/JP gear.
- The amount of BGs you have to run to buy a piece of Conquest/Honor gear.
- The amount of mats/gold you have to farm to make a piece of crafted gear.
- The amount of farming you have to do for Archaeology (I stopped leveling this after I saw what it was about. It's the most boring thing I've ever seen in WoW, and I hate RNG).


Grinding is actually easier for Cata than previous expansions. They made a few changes, such as VP being earned for the first 7 heroics, as opposed to 1 per day in WotLK.

As for grinding in general, we have been grinding since vanilla. It has always been the same old grinding for rep, grinding for badges/emblems, grinding dailies, grinding heroics, etc. We've been grinding for 7 years, all for the sake of incrementally better epic gear.

Grinding keeps us playing. Grinding is nothing new. I'm just surprised that you only feel ripped off now, when you've been doing it for such a long time.

Every game has some form of grinding. Grinding isn't a problem. But yes, WoW has taken it to a whole new level. But they do it just enough not to cause a lot of people to quit in frustration, and it is the formula that has make it the best MMO for years.

That is why I'm looking forward SW:TOR, because it focuses more on story rather than epics, and the grind to get them. I want WoW to be the game that I first played, where the game is about the journey from 1 to 60, where exploration and story is the focus. But why does WoW have to change it's recipe of success, just for the sake of taking a chance with players of my taste.

There's no way you can get WoW to lessen the grind, because it would mean forcing the devs to churn out more content to keep people from getting bored. They only give us just enough content, and just enough grind, to reduce costs and maximize profit.

I would agree that WoW is more grindy in Cata than it was in LK.

I don't think that's a good thing.

But all MMOs are grindy, and grinds, generally, aren't fun.

Grinds make it so that when you are in a BG looking for honor, you are possibly more concerned with how many more wins you need to get that next piece, instead of just playing the BG for fun. It's a mindset that the grind can cause, and that leads to people feeling like they are "working" instead of "playing".

The mat cost of crafted items seems to have increased in Cata, as well. My main (well, my LK raid main) is an alchemist. I think I made perhaps 15 or 20 flasks in Cata, and once I realized how much time (or gold) would be needed for each flask compared to in LK days, I stopped. That's just an herbalism grind. Early on most definitely it was "smarter" to sell the raw herbs and then buy a flask than it was to make your own flasks from your own collected herbs (likely due to inscription and leveling up of other alchemists).

I do have the "luxury" of not raiding in Cata (if you can call it luxury - I could pay money to change servers to raid, or I could not have accepted a better for me new work shift in order to keep raiding, but neither choice seems like a good one), so I feel less compelled to do the grind. I have 1 character that has been "raid ready" since 1st week of January (346 in every slot, unless crafted/rep/BoE 359's, and now quite a few 353's), but not "having" to raid has actually been a blessing in terms of the grindiness of WoW.

But..MMOs are grinds. I played Rift for a couple months, and it's the same over there (and while I never finished one, the end game rep grinds actually looked worse than WoWs). Maybe Grinds are the only thing developers can think of to keep people playing, I don't know. But I know that once you realize or think about something being "a grind", that something becomes less fun.

Take pvp honor grinding, for example. I enjoy pvp because it's not vs a scripted NPC enemy. There is most definitely a lot more variety to any BG than any raid boss encounter. Sure, some common themes emerge, but a BG is rarely "identical" like raiding.

But being an MMO, even pvp is gear-based over being skill-based. That makes it so that as soon as I'm on a character that is gear-challenged, compared to the enemy, it's less fun and a grind.

What I mean by that is I have 4 85s. My shaman has over 4k resil as resto, and my druid has over 4k resil as resto. My priest has around 1900 resil as disc, and my DK has around 2200 resil (currently unholy). At current gear-states, I feel like I'm simply pvping on both the shaman and druid, but if I go into a BG on the priest or DK I feel like I'm grinding honor (as I'm definitely less effective than many of my opponents simply due to gear). I have drastically less fun on the priest and DK, and would much rather the gear component be totally removed from pvp.

And to crafting grind, the insane mats cost of the 339 crafted pvp items is a joke. They should be, basically, free, and considered the equivalent of quest greens. But they were made to be high level (above 500) recipes, and as a result have very high costs for their actual current value.
