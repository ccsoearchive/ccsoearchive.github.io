This is where I don't follow you Elflow. The "theory" posted has nothing to do with the binary outcome of death or life. It is how one determines the average mitigation that blood shield will provide in a situation. Just like people will tell you 1% avoidance will stop 1% of the damage. This is how you derive the amount of damage that blood shield will stop. I'm not really sure where the disconnect is here. If you go through all that math, it will tell you that under conditions, x, y, and z, mastery will stop a certain percent of damage. It tells you nothing about the feel of the class, the healer perspective, the player skill during the encounter, etc. It tells you the average amount of damage your mastery will prevent.

If you want to post your own experiences on the matter, feel free. They are relevant because the entire game concept transcends numbers and largely boils down to personal opinions because the entire activity is based upon personal enjoyment. However, it is very difficult to quantify discrepancies in that realm. Numbers provide easily provable information. The numbers will tell you the worth of mastery in relation to others. It can support claims of avoidance versus mastery. It can support arguments about scaling. It can't tell you anything beyond that.

The problem is that most people do rely on mathematical evidence because it has such a quantitative measure. That is the reason I bothered posting this. I wanted to show the value of our mastery as the DK gears. That is the only thing it shows. It largely ignores the ever-present and real complaints made about diseases, rune-tetris, etc. Whether the person be in favor or against said complaints. They are the heart of the issue; the math, however, serves to show a tremendous flaw in Blizzard's thinking. 1- That DKs will scale correctly. 2- That a DK will keep up with mediocre DS use. That was the only reason I made the post. It quantitatively proves those assumptions to be false. The rest have to be proven and shown from the perspective of the user; which is indeed of great importance, but far harder to conclusively prove.
