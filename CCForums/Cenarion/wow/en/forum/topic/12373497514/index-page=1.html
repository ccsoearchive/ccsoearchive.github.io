So after a thought-provoking discussion in OOC tonight I decided to do a write-up on all the available information about the laws of the Kingdom of Stormwind.

For those of you who don't know who I am, I play the character of Foravin who is the King's Prosecutor for the Kingdom of Stormwind. He's also a member of the Stormwind Union (Formerly the Stormwind Watch).

I've taken it upon myself to educate myself on all the information we have out there about the lore behind the laws for Stormwind, as it is required to accurately play my character.

Please note that the total information available to use that is officially backed by Blizzard is small, and most of what I have to work off of are discussions and compilations from guilds across all the RP Servers on WoW. It isn't the best thing in the world to base these kind-of things off of, but again, I work with what I have.

If anyone has additional sources and citations not listed here, please post them. I would love to be able to read them.

I helped set up one trial before this latest one, a few years ago. It lasted a few hours and was not really enjoyed by anyone involved, even though we started with the best of intentions. That's why the Watch has settled all arrests via OOC negotiation ever since. I'm not going to say trials can't be done well, but after participating in two trials now, here are some barriers I've identified to replicating a real trial:

1. We don't have rules for trial proceedings
2. We don't have trained court officials
3. We have no case law
4. We don't have months to spend on each case
5. We don't have any way of ensuring that everyone who should be at a trial (especially witnesses, will show up for it)

To actually have fair trials that aren't chaotic, we need all of those things. That's not going to happen. Therefore, I think the best we can do is approximate the feel of a trial, with a WoW twist.

I believe the most important part of creating a trial that feels like one and is actually enjoyable, is having the participants agree on an outcome in advance. Given the limitations listed above, we simply don't have the capability to put on a trial that will fairly weigh all the evidence and arguments and render a just decision simply on the basis of what occurs in the trial. It's difficult enough even in reality. A trial that lasts even a few hours can become a drag, and it's even worse when it bogs down in disagreements over what should be happening and whether people are behaving appropriately in their roles (especially in the case of the attorneys and judge). I would argue that the only way to cut out all these problems is to agree on some basic rules and the outcome in advance. Then the participants can focus on the RP, and not worry about getting the huge number of details inherent to a trial correct. If the community would like, the Watch could create a draft of a simple trial procedure and rules for discussion.

To sum up, trials are something that could become a more regular occurrence, but only if we focus on replicating the atmosphere of a trial, and don't try to put on a full-blown one.
