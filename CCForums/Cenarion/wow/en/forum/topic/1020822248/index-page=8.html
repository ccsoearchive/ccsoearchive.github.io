You are confusing questioning with criticizing. However, my rebuttal in the form of a snarky summary was admittedly the wrong approach; all I've done is exacerbate the problem. So, I'll give this another shot.

It's creative writing, bordering on poetry (magnified in no small part thanks to the "poor" translation). Some people can't understand or appreciate art, and art often cannot be explained adequately. The meaning to each individual is interpretive and subjective. That said...

The "necro" is actually part of the story. This was written, as the preface outlines, long before Rogues had modern survivability tools. Rogues main mitigation was to remove themselves from combat with Vanish. Mana was in shorter supply than today, with small pools sometimes rationed amongst 40 people. This meant Rogues were an afterthought for healing spells, and relied on bandages instead. As a community, Rogues grew used to the idea of being ignored as outcasts in the shadows and having to fend for themselves - a small sacrifice for the betterment of everyone else.

The point is not just about "healing priority." While that is a considerable component, that is like saying Huckleberry Finn was just a story about a rafting trip; it's overly simplistic. This is a literary bridge between two groups, both in game and in real life.

On one hand, It is an olive branch from the powerful to the down-trodden. It is a recognition of plight and disenfranchisement, and an account of one lone person's efforts to remedy that unethical, culturally-ingrained inequity. Rogues often helped Healers more than vice versa, sometimes with a stun or even by stealing aggro, or simply by saving potential mana with bandaging. This was an ironic circumstance given Rogues' namesake and Healers' intended role as overseers and protectors. Why shouldn't Healers return the favor? This perspective inspired a revolution of sorts. Today it is unthinkable to let a raid member go entirely without heals.

On the other hand, it is an acknowledgement of a shared experience between isolated cultures in real life. Complaining about a lack of proper grammar as a value judgement is misguided and disrespectful; that is a significant part of why it is so impactful . How many languages are you fluent in? Were you inspired by the story of Hellen Keller, or did you think she had horrible pronunciation?

And don't say I'm reading too much into it. This is one of the greatest things ever written. Yet, again, I'm speaking for my personal interpretation and connection to the content. YMMV, but I encourage you to read it again.
