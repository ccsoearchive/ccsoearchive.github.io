I didn't even see this.....you really think Durumu's floor is purple? LOL! Oh and by the way, the maze is dark blue/black with purple under it. Go google a screenshot of Durumu maze and you'll see.


Durumu's floor is a different color than the fog/smoke stuff. However, if (totally hypothetically), you put it graphic settings on black-and-white, the shade difference for gray-scale would be so low that you would hardly be able to see where the boundaries are.

This is a problem because your brain has two different paths that visual information takes, and those systems do different things.

For example, the magnocellular cells in your vision system are more strongly implicated in motion processing and coarse outlines. This system doesn't process color, and can't really work that well when the gray-scale version of the fight wouldn't have distinctive boundaries for the maze. This system works best with black/white very distinct shades, and does less well when two shades of gray are more similar.

The parvocellular system, however, processes color (and the color of the floor and fog is different), and they do more detail processing. However, this is less helpful and slower information for detecting edge-boundaries for the maze and could potentially slow down reaction times.

So, having higher contrast in gray-scale shading allows your magnocellular system to help with the motion processing and giving you quick estimates of the coarse outline of the maze. Not having distinct luminance differences between figure/ground causes visual processing problems for that system. Thus, really bright on a dark background, or really dark on a light background, is much easier for our visual systems - and for the maze in particular, their choice of contrast differences was lacking.

Classes in medical school require you to memorize information and commit it to long-term memory. I ask students in my psychology classes to memorize huge lists of key term words for exams. I didn't say that we should never ask people to commit anything to long-term memory.

In fact, I've been arguing that it's okay to ask specialists doing hard-mode content to have to research, study, and commit info to long-term memory. In this case, they are making a huge commitment just like being in med school. In this case, you should have super complex fights. Just like in med school you are at a level where you meet the pre-requisite learning (taking all the required undergrad courses, having a high GPA, and doing well on standardized-tests).

However, should LFR have the same requirements as medical school and hard-mode raiding? That's a much more useful question. It's not all terribly black and white. I would argue that LFR raiding in WOW shouldn't require as much studying as a medical school class does (in some cases, Blizzard does a good job in keeping content at a reasonable level, and in some cases, they take steps too far over the other side, and sometimes it may be too easy).

If you think that every LFR fight should be as difficult as a medical school course, then I'm not sure we'll ever come to an agreement about boss difficulty.

For people who aren't in med school, however, they can choose other career paths that don't have the same demands and expert levels of knowledge. And thus LFR should stay at a level of not requiring expert levels of knowledge, but hard-mode raiding absolutely should.
