I think the fundamental issue isn't the number of mechanics but rather the belief that the everyday casual should not have to work to gain end game content gear or achieves.

The casual raider (and I use that term "raider" lightly) expect the gear to be handed to them with little effort. How many AFKs have you sighted recently? Be honest.

I would be happily surprised if hardly any of them look at the DJ at all before jumping in. As such Blizz adjusts the damage level to accomodate them and they breeze through.

The OP has demonstrated that the number of mechanics is beyond the memory capacity (Short Term and Working) to learn up front. For LFR its not an issue as Blizzard have mamed the mechanics to allow for it.

But here is the thing, for Normal/Heroic raiding - no guild - expects to be able to waltz in and steamroll the bosses (Well maybe Method or BL :P )

Each raider spends the time (during raid nights) either from wiping to the mechanic and learning how to beat it or reads up what they can and progressively wipe by wipe you work out how to defeat it. You then move onto the next mechanic. Eventually you commit it all to memory - and with a final flurry of effort - u get the kill.

From ToT, Horridon is a perfect example. You learn each door, you execute the strategy you developed for that door and you get the kill in the execute phase.

This is the essence of raiding imho. It doesn't matter how many mechanics there are. Its the learning and application of that learning successfully which gives you the satisfaction when the boss drops.

Defeating the mechanics is the best part of normal/heroic raiding. Its frustrating as hell until you get there - but its worth it. And if you don't think so - then why are you raiding?

Hey Liss, hope you see this:

Mods like BigWigs and DBM do lessen the burden of memorizing encounters, but there are other ways of storing and recalling information. Lets take Horridon, there's a lot of bullet points in the journal for this encounter. In an actual raid environment, you have assignments and mechanics to obey.

There are 5 phases in Horridon. Each phase has their own set of adds with similar spells. Rather than memorize every single spell, we can "group up" similar spell effects.

The first 4 phases share the following:
- "Don't stand in the fire."
- "Dispel."
- "Dinomancer"
- "Three (3) primary adds"
- "Many secondary adds"
- "Horridon"

From this, we can start going into details for each individual phase of the encounter:
- You don't need to interrupt on 2 of 4 doors.
- You need to dispel Magic, then Poison, then Disease, then Curse.
- You only need to get the Dinomancer down to 50% on every door.
- Add priorities never change
- Secondary adds shouldn't be focused down, but instead cleaved.
- Horridon management never changes from Door1 through Door4.

From these details we can build assignments. And from assignments we can "forget" many aspects of the encounter that do not concern us:
- Interrupt assignments (Door2, Door4, Dinomancer)
- Dispel assignments (Hex of Confusion mostly)
- Correct handling of Charge

As I play moonkin, my assignments for this encounter:
- Don't mess up Charge (Horridon)
- Don't stand in the fire.
- Dispel Hex of Confusion, Poison Bolt Volley if someone messes up their interrupt (though a Monk Revival is best)
- DPS Dinomancer to 50%, then Big Adds, then clean up small adds (dot'd small adds).

When I play my Warrior, my assignments are:
- Interrupt "skull" on Door2, Door4
- Don't stand in the fire.
- DPS Dinomancer to 50%, then Big Adds, then clean up small adds (dot'd small adds).
- Click Orb of Control to force the phase and stop the flow of secondary adds.

I don't need to memorize all the mechanics of an encounter, but I should know everything I may need to handle. This tier is mostly about execution. Some DPS checks exist, but proper strategy and execution will increase the DPS of your group, hopefully.

One thing many players forget is to stay calm. Just play your specc correctly, don't stress over the details too much. As many top tier players will tell you, "It's not over until the boss is dead." Keep mashing buttons in a somewhat-coordinated manner because a poorly handled Torrent of Ice may completely annihilate the ranged pile. Whether or not the fight has a lot of mechanics, you deal with them as they come. Some players may not know how to react to encounter mechanics, but that comes with experience. Again, just take a step back and understand what YOU need to do in order to help kill the boss.

Hell, I'm supposed pretty good. I make mistakes quite often, but I fix my mistakes and play better the next time. There is such a thing a "trying too hard." Once you get comfortable with the encounter and understand what you need to do, the boss will die. We had a few hiccups on Heroic Primordius, but managed to kill it after fixing a single issue with the Multi-DOT'ing group (damage allocation). Literally on the next pull it was a comfortable kill, and a damn near flawless one at that.

Many of the fights in the dungeon journal have a lot of bullet points that do not apply to every player. And largely many of the bullet points on fights are just more detailed explanations of things.

For example on Primordius there are 8 mutations that are listed in the dungeon journal. But no player actually needs to memorize all 8. Blizz could have easily just had 2 "A helpful mutation that increases Crit, Mastery, or Haste by 10%, or stats by 5%" and "A harmful mutation that reduces Crit, Mastery, or Haste by 20% or stats by 10%".

Additionally on Jin'Rohk as you brought up there are 14 bullet points.

But a good number of those bullet points are not additional information. In reality there are far less things to know.

1. Thundering throw - has 1 bullet point in DJ
2. Pools that DPS/Heals stand in and tanks stand out of (or in LFR that everyone stands in) - has 3 bullet points in DJ
3. Tank Swap debuff - has 2 bullet points in DJ
4. Focused Lightning that needs to be dropped on edges of room where pools do not go, do not overlap - has a grand total of 6 bullet points.
5. Lightning storm where players need to stack in mid away from pools, pools become bad afterwards - has 2 bullet points.

So sure, if you need to know word for word every mechanic that is listed then it is a bit much, but really the dungeon journal could have 5 bullet points for Jin'rokh and still be able to describe all his mechanics. Honestly no more than 4 of those affect any actual role. Tanks do not need to know the mechanics of Focused Lightning, DPS and heals do not need to know the mechanics of the 2 tank swaps.

In the past Blizzard has had bosses that where hard not because of mechanics, but because of sheer damage output. These bosses where long, zerging fights, and made a majority of the bosses. As time progressed players demanded more complex fights, and as the game design and vision evolved, so did fight design. The difficulty in the encounter became less about the damage output, and more about following the fight mechanics to survive and win.

With this in mind, it is important to note that currently, in LFR most of the mechanics that require a lot of memory are mute. They are dumbed down, and while still dangerous are nowhere near as unforgiving as Normal or Heroic raiding.

Normal mode raiding shouldn't be a pushover, and it requires effective coordination and paying attention in order to down bosses, often including the avoidance of mechanics and strong DPS.

Heroic raiding is often completely dependent upon mechanics making the encounter difficult, with high end raiders being forced to learn, analyze, and follow fight mechanics.

Finally, the player base demanded less patchwork style fights and more fights with mechanics, so now as blizzard delivers there is growing complaints about the mechanics of the fights we demanded? This is hilarious.
