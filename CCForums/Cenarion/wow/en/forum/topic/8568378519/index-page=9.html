This wouldn't work nearly so well if bosses didn't use their abilities predictably exactly on cooldown. It would still be *some* use to know when the ability comes off cooldown, but you couldn't preactivate a cooldown of your own if you know the boss might wait up to another 10 or 15 seconds to actually use that ability. I think this would help reduce reliance on addons. When a major ability actually starts, even the standard AI will let you know about it (heck, half the bosses even call the attack), so if it's designed so that you can react to it after it starts, then you don't need an addon to perform adequately.

And of course some major abilities revolve around a boss resource that anyone can look at at any time - stone guard, empress, sha, tortos, for example.

Anyway, I think the OP's numbers are slightly inflated by the fact that the DJ includes abilities nobody can really actively do anything about, as well as including all the mechanics where any given role only needs to know/perform part of them. (And in some cases, like Ji-kun, not even everyone in that role needs to perform that mechanic.)

Although I have to admit, I'm missing the first week of LFR Halls of Flesh-Shaping because I couldn't find the time to research the fights before jumping into them, and didn't want to go in blind and wipe the raid (my experience with other ToT wings on the first week is that not everyone is this considerate, which usually results in very firmly disproving the myth that you don't need to know/do mechanics to succeed in LFR, but I don't want to be that guy). So maybe there is something to this point...

I'm not entirely certain if this thread is complaining about LFR or normal modes having too many mechanics, as it seems that the OP thinks both are out of line. However, with that said, there are a variety of higher order cognitive abilities that can be used to deal with this bloat: The two most pronounced have already been discussed in this thread.

On page 4, Slashlove brought up the idea of "Pattern Recognition". This can be employed in many encounters in order to ease the burden of memory constraints. This can be employed in an encounter like Blade Lord (Tempest->Wind Step->Tempest->Blade Tempest or Unseen Strike, repeats).

Another example from Cyous on page 8 is the concept of chunking. Chunking allows you to group similar mechanics together in memory tasks. His example of Horridon is one of the best ones I can think of for this (Each door has 3 elite adds and multiple spawning small adds. Each big add has a void zone analogue and requires a dispel. Dinomancers should be killed to advance. Jalakk is highest priority.) That essentially puts Horridon at 6 mechanics, which is in line with typical memory capabilities.

While somewhat unfair, many mechanics are recycled older mechanics, and when learning a fight, those that have a history in raiding can just recall how to deal with the previous incarnation of the mechanic and have already mastered it. This is evident in that World in Flames (Ragnaros) is analogous to Heigen's dance. To a lesser extent, Regali's Lightning Storm is too.

Another important thing to note is that in non-LFR scenarios, you can delegate who's doing what to free other people's memory up. (Amber Shaper, Person 1 calls out 10 seconds before Amber Monstrosity casts Amber Explosion, Person 2 calls out when 4 seconds before Fling happens.)

And even beyond that, many mechanics are unnecessary to learn for most roles. People have been saying this since the first page, but it hasn't been met with a response outside of asking how to know what mechanics are important and which ones aren't (which is denoted in the dungeon journal). If you're asking which mechanics are important on LFR, then you'll need to experience the fight to learn, because allowing LFR to have all of the mechanics, but dumbed down, is very useful.

I agree with the OP in that the demands of WoW raid fights now, for whatever reason-be it the increasing proliferation of boss mods, the increasing general skill base of raiders, whatever-have, in this tier, passed beyond the realm of complex and entered the realm of overly complicated for no apparent reason other than "add this cheap mechanic right now to make the fight harder." Jin'rokh is in theory a simple fight, until you get into it and see things like the lightning balls being put on one of your healers just before lightning storm goes off and you wonder who just thought a cheap shot like that was a good idea, or the sheer genius of the tank debuff move ALSO doing raid wide damage. Every fight has one or two abilities added to it that seem to be there just to make things much complicated, and removing them wouldn't make the fights cakewalks.

Throne of Thunder is the largest raid since ICC, and I'd hazard a guess that a quarter of ToT has more encounter mechanics to learn than ALL of ICC. That's faintly ridiculous. The raid design team is mistaking complicated for complexity, and forgetting that sometimes all a fight needs is one single, well designed ability to make it a challenge. Look at the Lich King. The raid wiper wasn't "six people getting killed at once by an AOE while the tank dies from mistiming a taunt on the fourteen adds that attacked during subphase 2." It was "oops, stood in Defile." I think Blizzard took the criticism of the relative simplicity of Dragon Soul-and yes, it was relative, on servers where there aren't a lot of heavy progression raiding, people were still earning Destroyer's End when the buff was at its max-and in typical fashion let the pendulum swing too far the other way.

And I'm not even going to get into the brain rattling stupidity of the purple on purple with purple telling you where to go of Durumu's maze. That's a special breed of stupid on its own.
