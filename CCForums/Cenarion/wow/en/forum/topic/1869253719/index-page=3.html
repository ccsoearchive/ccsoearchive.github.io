Great thread Waniou!

I would like to comment on diminishing returns though:

One big misconception is that there's a point where DR "kicks in" or "kicks in harder", along the lines of "past 20% you're getting almost nothing from dodge due to DR, so stop there"

The important thing to remember here is that Avoidance DR causes your survivability gains to be linear

Before DR was implemented, the statement was "Each point of dodge rating is more valuable than the last"

Because people read the word DIMINISHING, they thought the statement really meant "At some point, getting more dodge rating will not provide you any benefit"

After DR was implemented, the statement is actually "Each point of dodge rating is equally valuable than the rest"

Source? Your general point that the point of DR was to stop avoidance from insane scaling, but is the resulting curve actually linear?


Think of it this way:

Going from 97% to 98% avoidance reduces all incoming damage by a third, going from 98% to 99% avoidance reduces all incoming damage by half, and going from 99% to 100% avoidance reduces all incoming damage infinitely.

Prior to DR, the amount of dodge rating you needed to get each of those percentage points was always the same, but the 99th percentage point was way more valuable than the 98th percentage point, and the 100th percentage point was infinitely more valuable.

Post-DR, the cost is adjusted such that the 99th percentage point is twice as expensive as the 98th to reflect its value, and that the 100th percentage point is infinitely more expensive to reflect the same. Infinitely in this case meaning that you can never actually reach it.

When considering amounts of dodge rating as opposed to dodge percentages, adding 100 dodge rating is going to result in just as much damage reduction as the previous 100 dodge rating - your percentage goes up by less to reflect the fact that as your baseline amount of dodge increases, you need less actual dodge to gain the same damage reduction.

Yeto,

No, and this is a known fact--Blizzard has said that the combat system is single role. That would be a dual role system.

When you are in combat against an enemy, the game gauges each hit against your avoidance/block stats and.. Using the example I used earlier.. against a mob of the same level

If you have 12% dodge and 15% parry and 30% block the game rolls a number..

If it is 1-5, the mob misses
If it is 6-17, the attack is dodged
If it is 18-32, the attack is parried
if it is 33-62, the attack is blocked and a portion of damage is done to you.

It makes no difference in what order these are in from a practical standpoint, even if block was before miss on the hit table, it would simply be:

If it is 1-30, the attack is blocked and portion of damage is done to you.
if it is 31-35, the attack misses.

This is mathematically impossible.

Suppose your combat table looks like this:

25% Dodge, 25% Parry, 50% Hit

Suppose 100 Parry rating at this point gives you 1% Parry. To ensure that your condition is met (100 Parry rating always exactly as valuable), the next 100 Parry rating will give you slightly less — say 0.95% Parry.

Now you equip the Insanely Overpowered Dodge Trinket, which makes your combat table like this.

73% Dodge, 25% Parry, 2% Hit

But since the dodge and parry DR curves are independent, your first 100 parry rating still gives you 1% and the next 100 parry rating still gives you 0.95%.

But in this case that second 0.95% is much, much more valuable than the first 1%!

You can't win. Whatever numbers you choose to make things work at a given dodge/miss, will not work ay any other dodge/miss amount.
