Your Initiation - Foreword (J1)

The first spec on your left, Assassination has classically been a bit of an oddball spec. Originally following the same path as the Arcane spec for Mages, in the early days of the old talent point system, most Rogues only ventured into the tree for Seal Fate, a talent that gave extra combo points on critical combo point generating moves like Backstab and Sinister Strike, or for Cold Blood, a now deprecated talent that used to ensure a critical strike on your next ability. As it went through expansions it slowly began to gain traction until Cataclysm, when Assassination was able to hold it's own against the other two specs.

Now, Assassination is as competitive, if not more so, than Combat and Sub. The rotation is simple and requires less management than the other two specs and pulls exceptional numbers. Assassination has come a long way from it's origins.

This compendium will help you identify common Rogue terms, maximize your gear, talents and glyphs, and perfect your strategy in raid encounters during patch 6.0.3: Warlords of Draenor. Most of the advice in this guide will focus on preparing you for Blackrock Foundry (Which releases on February 2nd.) but should apply just as well to previous raid tiers and other content. If you have any questions or suggestions, please, feel free to leave them in a reply to this thread! I'll get back to you as soon as possible. I also have a Twitter! Just send a tweet to @JoTheRogue to chat!

Patch 6.2 Changes for Assassination Rogues:

  • Cloak of Shadows now has a 90-second cooldown (up from 60 seconds)
  • Envenom now deals 30% more damage.
  • Mutilate now deals 30% more damage.
  • Deadly Throw now reduces the target's movement speed by 70% (up from 50%) but no longer interrupts spellcasting when used with 5 combo points.
  • Shuriken Toss now deals 53% more autoattack damage. Additionally, fixed a bug that caused hits made with offhand weapons to deal 15% more damage than intended.

    Your Build - Talents and Glyphs (J3)

    Talents

    First up, we have the talents! Every tier of talents will begin with a Listen up! section that summarizes the talent selections for that tier. If you want more information, then read on for a more in depth and critical analysis of each talent.

    ~Level 15 - Tier 1~

    Listen up!

    Nightstalker yields to Shadow Focus in terms of damage but could be good if you can make critical use of the speed bonus, Subterfuge can be good for emergency add control but requires Vanish and isn't a DPS increase, Shadow Focus yields the best damage and is good for anything. Do bear in mind that, no matter which you choose, this tier will have very little impact in your overall damage.

    Hungry for more? Read on, brothers and sisters in the shadows...


    Nightstalker is mostly used as an extra speed boost to get from point A to B if you take it. The damage bonus isn't as good as the potential damage of saved energy from Shadow Focus. Unless you can benefit often from the speed bonus (Rarely.), Shadow Focus is going to be the better choice.
    Example Usages: None.

    Subterfuge is the worst option you can take for raw damage output. Mutilate is superior to Ambush as an opener and you don't need stealth to Mutilate, so Subterfuge won't be increasing your damage. There's possibly some uses for it on select fights depending on your job and/or strategy. If adds are becoming a problem, such as the Shaman adds on Galakras, it wouldn't be terrible to Vanish and CS 3-4 of them. (Although it would be better at that point to reassess what your fellows are killing and interrupting.)
    Example Usages: None.

    Shadow Focus allows you a nearly free Mutilate. (Why Mutilate and not Ambush? More on that in the tips and tricks section!) The energy you save more than makes up for the extra damage you would get with Nightstalker. Don't forget that it also reduces the cost of any ability that costs energy in Stealth. Spam that Burst of Speed!
    Example Usages: Any fight.

    ~Level 30 - Tier 2~

    Listen up!

    Deadly Throw is for ranged kiting and interrupting if no other players can do so, Nerve Strike is fantastic anytime you are stunning something, Combat Readiness is good for add tanking and emergencies.

    Hungry for more? Read on, brothers and sisters in the shadows...


    Deadly Throw is another one of those niche talents in PvE, similar to Nightstalker. In general, you don't want enemies slowed in PvE, so the tank can move them easily as need be. You also shouldn't be spending extended amounts of time out of range of a boss. In order for this talent to ever be a damage increase, you would need to be far enough from a boss for long enough that you would energy cap. That doesn't happen often. The interrupt has some possible uses but there aren't many fights that demand so many interrupts that someone else cannot assist on, or that would merit running out of melee range to do so. That being said, it could have some interesting uses on certain fights if there's an add that needs interrupting and it's an emergency.
    Example Usages: None.

    Nerve Strike is more of an add fight talent. In general, you can't stun bosses, so your hopes of being a tank saver will have to be realized some other way. This talent is great for, say, the trash before Garrosh as a 50% damage reduction on an enemy for 6 seconds is very powerful.
    Example Usages: Any fight where you will be stunning adds.

    Combat Readiness is a great talent. Netting you a possible 30 seconds of 50% magic and physical damage reduction that stacks with Elusiveness, this usually works much better than Nerve Strike or Deadly Throw. The only problem... Is triggering it. Here's how it works: for 20 seconds, you have a buff that, when hit by a melee attack, will give you 1 stack of 10% damage reduction, up to a total of 5 stacks. Note that it only triggers on direct melee attacks, meaning you have threat on an enemy. Generally, this is more of a fallback talent for when you can't use Prey on the Weak, but you won't be losing much if you decide not to switch. It could have some merits if you need to emergency tank for some reason, especially when combined with Elusiveness, but if you're going to get melee'd, why not just use Evasion and avoid all the hits?
    Example Usages: None.

    ~Level 45 - Tier 3~

    Listen up!

    Cheat Death is a great talent for most encounters and shines on fights with heavy or unpredictable incoming damage, Leeching Poison is good for fights with low, constant and predictable damage, Elusiveness is decent for AoE damage but shines on reducing single target damage.

    Hungry for more? Read on, brothers and sisters in the shadows...


    Cheat Death has a rather long history. Introduced in Burning Crusade, it was the first talent of it's kind and hasn't really changed much since then, save for an ill-fated relationship with resilience. It still retains a lot of the bite it had back then and is a solid choice for this tier. The downside is that if you're pretty confident of what's going on in a fight and can avoid most damage, then the value of Cheat Death drops. It's great for progression, any fight with some sort of instant death mechanic, unpredictable damage and/or damage that Feint alone has a hard time getting you through.
    Example Usages: Any fight.

    Leeching Poison is a good choice now with the recent change to being a flat 10% Leech. Leeching Poison works best with fights that have small amounts of incoming damage or damage that is very predictable. It also works well for fights that you have a firm grasp of, such as those you have on farm, but Cheat Death will still work better if it's a fight with heavy or unpredictable damage. The Shiv effect is okay but you shouldn't be using it unless you're in serious danger of death. (Which shouldn't be happening too often, otherwise you're better off with Cheat Death or Elusiveness.)
    Example Usages: Gorefiend.

    Elusiveness is a fantastic talent. Feint is already a great ability, even if it wasn't in the past, and Elusiveness makes it even better. Because, let's face it, you are going to take damage in a raid. So, any time you are taking significant damage, whether AoE or single target, it behooves you to use Feint. It is worth noting that the 30% damage reduction from Elusiveness stacks multiplicatively with Feint, meaning you only get a 65% reduction in AoE damage, so it isn't the greatest for straight AoE damage situations. The 30% stacks additively with other forms of universal damage reduction though, such as Smoke Bomb. It's worth noting, however, that Elusiveness is only valuable when you know damage is coming and that you can mitigate it. Because the average raid has plenty of ways to deal with predictable damage, this talent isn't generally as useful as Feign Death.
    Example Usages: Any fight where you will take significant single target damage.

    ~Level 60 - Tier 4~

    Listen up!

    Cloak and Dagger is only good for long range infrequent target swaps, Shadowstep is fantastic for most movement, Burst of Speed good for periods of extended movement or moving to spots without a target.

    Hungry for more? Read on, brothers and sisters in the shadows...


    Cloak and Dagger is the oddball of this tier. It's not a very old talent, but in the past it was more of a mainstay of Sub. Now, no one really wants it, and for good reason. Think of it this way: Cloak and Dagger is like a Shadowstep that requires you to Vanish or otherwise be in Stealth to use. Now ask yourself, "Do I want an instant teleport behind my enemy that is tied to Vanish, which is on a two minute cooldown, or do I want the same package but on a separate 20 second cooldown?". The answer should be pretty obvious. And hey, Shadowstep can be used on allies to boot! The only real merit to Cloak and Dagger is the 40 yard range, which could be good if you find yourself needing to switch targets no more than every two minutes or if you need to get to an enemy that you normally can't reach with Shadowstep, but these situations are so specific that it's unliekly you will ever find yourself in them. This is one of the few talents that I advise you to never take.
    Example Usages: None.

    Shadowstep was originally a Sub ability that was intended to ease getting behind an enemy to start your rotation and even added a damage bonus to Ambush and Garrote. Over time, it lost the Stealth requirement and come Mists of Pandaria, it lost the damage bonus at the cost of being available to all Rogues. It's still a fantastic talent however. Not on the GCD, costs no energy and has a fairly short CD for it's purposes. This will generally work the best out of this tier due to the lack of an energy cost and the fact that it's instant movement, unlike Burst of Speed. Notably, Shadowstep lets you directly skip many ground-based mechanics rather than having to run around them However, on any fight where you need to move more often than once every 20 seconds, Burst of Speed starts to edge out. This is also true on any fight where you need to move to a location where there is no enemy or ally to Shadowstep to or, in the case of some fights such as Ko'ragh, you don't want to be directly behind the boss, just in melee range. However, in those situations you can just Sprint, and Blizzard tries to avoid very heavy movement fights for melee in general.
    Example Usages: Any boss.

    Burst of Speed has a lot of potential to be an extremely useful ability and is one of my favorite talents. Unfortunately, there's just not a lot of movement on most fights that merit Burst of Speed over Shadowstep and even when you do need to move and Shadowstep is on CD or otherwise unavailable, you can Sprint. It's worth noting that there are quite a few snares out there with some other detrimental effect tacked on. Burst of Speed is great for removing these debuffs as well.
    Example Usages: Running around on Archimonde, getting to all the spirits in Gorefiend's belly.

    ~Level 75 - Tier 5~

    Listen up!

    Prey on the Weak is fantastic for raid focusing stunnable adds, Internal Bleeding is great for solo killing stunnable adds, Dirty Tricks has no uses.

    Hungry for more? Read on, brothers and sisters in the shadows...


    Prey on the Weak suffers from the same problem as Nerve Strike in that there aren't many boss encounters that allow for stuns. However, on a fight like Galakras, it's an overall raid damage increase to Kidney Shot an enemy if all your DPS are focusing on it. 10% increased damage taken for 6 seconds for all of your DPS is going to outweigh an Envenom. The downside is that your stun (and therefore damage increase) is subject to DRs, so your 4-6 second stun can easily be cut in half. Be sure to let your raid leader and other raid members know if you plan on using this! This talent works fantastically when paired with Nerve Strike.
    Example Usages: Any fight with stunnable adds that need to be focused down.

    Internal Bleeding is new with Warlords of Draenor and is also the third in a trend of talents that can't be used on raid bosses. Like all the talents on this tier, it focuses on your crowd control abilities, abilities that cannot be used on bosses under normal circumstances. The damage per CP with this talent is pretty darn meaty, much more than an Envenom, without including increased poison application. If there's an add you can stun and need to kill it yourself or with one or two other people, this talent proves to be pretty strong. If you're stunning something with more than three other people attacking it though, you should probably use Prey on the Weak.
    Example Usages: Padding your own damage meters. (You naughty Rogue!)

    Dirty Tricks is a fantastic talent for both PvP and doing solo content, but not so in raids. Gouging only turns off the attacks of the caster, not those of the rest of the raid, so that renders this talent fairly useless. I'm sure you could find some sort of use for it, but you'd have to be very clever and have some amount of luck. The Blind aspect of it is decent, but I'm not sure why you'd attack something you need to CC. The loss of the energy cost is negligible.
    Example Usages: None.

    ~Level 90 - Tier 6~

    Listen up!

    Shuriken Toss is pretty much useless, Marked for Death is great for encounters with adds being focuses and dying quickly, Anticipation is best for every other situation.

    Hungry for more? Read on, brothers and sisters in the shadows...


    Shuriken Toss is an interesting talent, focusing on "breaking the rules" as it is said, giving Rogues a ranged damage option. Used primarily as a filler for when you can't be in stabbing range, it's had a rather turbulent history since it's been implemented. Unfortunately, it doesn't have much of a use considering that most fights are designed to allow melee to attack something at all times. The other two talents are of much more benefit.
    Example Usages: None.

    Marked for Death isn't a bad talent and theoretically could be better than Anticipation for this tier. However, you'd have to execute your rotation with machine-like precision to devalue Anticipation far enough for Marked for Death to outweigh it - on a single target, that is. On any fight with multiple targets that will be dying rather quickly, Marked for Death proves to be very useful.
    Example Usages: Hellfire Assault, any fight with rapidly dying adds.

    Anticipation is a great talent that increases your efficiency magnificently. Basically, any combo points you accrue past the 5 point cap is stored as a stacking buff called Anticipation. Whenever you use a hostile combo point finisher (Envenom, Crimson Tempest, Rupture, Kidney Shot and Deadly Throw) your Anticipation charges are consumed to provide you with the same amount of combo points on your current target as charges you had. No more wasted combo points and the freedom to use 5 combo point finishers every time without fear of wasting CPs because your Mutilate or Dispatch decided to crit. It also helps a lot with the T15 4-piece, which overloads you with energy.
    Example Usages: Any fight without quickly dying adds being focused down.

    ~Level 100 - Tier 7~

    Listen up!

    Venom Rush is a good choice for most situations and is best for any fight that regularly has more than one target, Shadow Reflection is best on single target fights but has a bit of a learning curve and Death from Above is an iffy talent that isn't really a DPS increase or decrease.

    Hungry for more? Read on, brothers and sisters in the shadows...


    Venom Rush is a good talent that is suited to all encounters. On multiple targets, 10-15% increased energy regeneration is a pretty good bonus. Even on a single target fight, 5% increased regeneration is nothing to sneeze at. The bonus energy is pretty good too as it gives you a larger pool for Vendetta and the opening of the fight. It also lets you reengage faster for fights that have a lull in, since you'll likely be energy capping in those cases anyways.
    Example Usages: Any fight, shines more on fights with multiple targets up frequently such as Xhul'horac and Hellfire High Council.

    Shadow Reflection sounds really cool in theory. After all, who DOESN'T want a clone that does what you do? That makes you 100% better at whatever it is you're doing! Unfortunately, it doesn't work out that well in practice. A big problem with Shadow Reflection is that it doesn't gain the benefit of procs or buffs like we do and it doesn't apply poisons. That means it's damage is pretty much poop as it doesn't benefit from the Envenom buff or Venomous Wounds. That being said, it's still basically free damage and can make a noticeable impact if utilized at the right moments. The execution has to be done just right however or you won't make much of a difference.
    Example Usages: Iron Reaver, Fel Lord Zakuun, any single target fight or fight without frequent access to 2+ targets.

    Death from Above is the weakest of this tier of talents. Despite looking wicked awesome, Death from Above neither excels nor fails in terms of single target damage. As it works, it's basically an amped up Envenom and you should treat it as such for your single target and multi target rotation, replacing Envenom every 20 seconds. The problem with it is that the animation loses you 2-3 seconds of auto attacking, which more or less cancels out the increased Envenom damage as auto attacks make up quite a bit of our damage through poison applications. The second issue is that it prevents action for the animation time and then moves you to the target. In 2-3 seconds, something could easily go wrong and you can't react during that time. The boss could also move unpredictably, causing issues with your positioning.
    Example Usages: Looking really cool.

    Glyphs

    Next we have glyphs! I'm only going to list major glyphs worth considering at the moment. This doesn't mean that you're going to be terrible unless you only choose these glyphs, as glyphs don't have as much of an impact on performance as talents, so feel free to experiment! I may add a section on minor glyphs in the future, but it's not a huge priority as most of them have no affect on performance and those that do have a minuscule impact. Similar to the talents section, I'll start with a Listen up! section.

    ~Major Glyphs~

    Listen up!

    There are only few glyphs I heavily recommend. Glyph of Energy gives you more energy to get your rotation started, Glyph of Vendetta is a DPS increase as long as you can stay on target for at least 24 seconds of Vendetta and Glyph of Disappearance lets you Vanish more often which means more cheap Mutilates and more damage.

    Hungry for more? Read on, brothers and sisters in the shadows...


    Glyph of Energy is a flat increase to our energy, bringing us to 140 or 155 with Venom Rush. A higher energy cap usually doesn't mean more damage but it does allow you to get your rotation started faster. It also gives you more damage anytime there's a break in the fight and you have nothing to attack, such as intermission phases. Bear in mind that, should you find yourself always under 120 energy during a fight, this glyph isn't doing much. Still, it's a glyph that can increase your DPS, and those are rare. That being said, this glyph is the weakest of the three recommended glyphs so if you find yourself in need of another glyph for a specific encounter, this should be the first one to go.

    Glyph of Disappearance is a great glyph, giving you a cheap Mutilate every minute. Most uses of Vanish in raids don't rely on being stealthed after Vanishing, so you don't really lose much by taking it. All in all, this is a pretty straightforward DPS increase.

    Glyph of Feint is pretty straightforward - get more bang for your buck. On almost every fight, you should be using Feint, but this glyph will only improve Feint's efficiency for fights where you're chaining Feint back to back or when damage is coming in for longer than 5 seconds but not long enough to warrant another Feint use.

    Glyph of Smoke Bomb is a glyph that, similar to the Feint glyph, gives you more bang for your buck. This way your raid can absorb that yummy performance enhancing smoke for 2 seconds longer! This glyph is good if you're using Smoke Bomb regularly.

    Glyph of Vendetta is a fantastic glyph for most fights. There are a number of factors which can make this glyph a damage increase or a damage decrease, including trinket procs, raid buffs, potions etc. In general though, as long as you can sit on your target for at least 24 out of the 30 seconds glyphed Vendetta will last, it's an increase. In a perfect world, you only need one more second for it to be an increase, but there are a lot of factors to throw around so 24 is the safe bet. If for some reason you cannot attack the target for longer than that, leave it unglyphed.

    Glyph of Energy Flows is an interesting talent, turning your defensive cooldown into a slightly offensive one. This doesn't give you an excuse to try and tank mobs but it can be a pretty decent choice if the encounter has an ability you can dodge. This usually means physical AoE damage the boss or adds do but can also mean anytime when you need to tank an add or boss as part of an encounter or in an emergency.

    Glyph of Recovery is great if you're using Recuperate, which you should be doing if near death or under heavy damage. "But Jo! That means I'll lose damage!" While this may be true, it's also true that you'll be doing no damage if dead, and lower damage is better than no damage. Don't go crazy with the Recuperates though. Try to save them when using this glyph for when your other tools that don't affect your DPS, such as Cloak and Feint, just aren't doing enough.

    Glyph of Cloak of Shadows grants our low CD magic-get-out-of-jail-free-card some physical mitigation as well. Unfortunately, this glyph isn't the greatest as we often want to save Cloak for actual magic effects. Besides, we already have ways to mitigate physical damage - Evasion, Feint and our talents.

    Glyph of Sprint isn't bad; After all, it lets you get from point A to point B faster so you can continue to kill things. But it usually isn't necessary thanks to Shadowstep. If you find yourself using Sprint on cooldown, it may be worth picking up.

    Your Gear - Stats, Enchanting and Gemming (J4)

    Here we'll be going over what to do with your gear in terms of enchanting and gemming. But first things first: There's not much of a point to such information if you don't know what each stat does for us and how it interacts. So first, I'm give a brief overview of what everything does for us.

    Stats

    Agility is our primary stat and what should be on all your gear. It increases our attack power but sadly no longer increases our dodge or crit chance. In general, you're going to want as much of this as you can get your thieving little hands on.

    Mastery Rating is a unique stat that does something different for everyone based on their class and spec. For Assassination Rogues, mastery increases all the damage we deal through poison effects. This affects Deadly Poison, Wound Poison, Envenom and Venomous Wounds. On an average fight, poison is going to represent a lot of your damage, in the area of around 60-70%, so Mastery is a pretty valuable stat.

    Haste Rating increases the speed at which we can attack. It affects our weapon swing time (or stab time I suppose) and our energy regeneration. It also increases the tick rate of DoTs and HoTs as well as lowering the GCD for many other classes, but this is not the case for Rogues. The GCD cannot go lower than 1 second, and Rogues have a base GCD of 1 second while many other classes have a base GCD of 1.5 seconds. Haste has classically been a popular stat for Rogues, since a lot of our damage comes from passive weapon strikes but has fallen in value for Assassination due to changes in how we gain energy.

    Critical Strike Rating increases our chance at doing double damage with any of our abilities. This includes our DoTs, such as Rupture and Deadly Poison. Crits with Mutilate, Dispatch and Fan of Knives generate an extra combo point, meaning Crit can help to smooth our rotation. Thankfully, we have quite a bit of it naturally thanks to the new passive all Agility classes got to make up for losing Agility giving crit. Our class trinket off of Archimonde causes the value of crit to plummet.

    Multistrike Rating gives all of our damaging and healing abilities a chance equal to your Multi percent to deal two extra attacks or heals for 30% of value. This means that if you have 5% Multi (Which you have without any gear.) then when you Dispatch, a number is generated between 1 and 100 twice. If one number is between 1 and 5, you get a 30% extra damage or healing proc with it's own chance to crit. If BOTH numbers are between 1 and 5, you get it twice! This tends to work similar to Crit in that it's a chance at extra damage. Some classes and specs have special effects related to Multistrike but Assassination does not. Note that percentage based heals, such as Recuperate, cannot proc Multistrike.

    Versatility Rating improves all of our damage and healing as well as giving us some damage mitigation. Versatility is kind of a jack of all trades, master of none stat so it doesn't have much value since it doesn't impact our DPS quite as much as other stats. It's also not as valuable as Rogues as it is for other specs as Versatility rating does not affect percentage based heals, which throws Recuperate out of the window.

    Stat Priority

    In general, your stat priority as an Assassination Rogue will be as follows:

    Agility > Mastery > Critical Strike > Multistrike > Versatility > Haste

    Note that these are static values. In reality, your stats will interact in such a way that gaining or losing in one area will impact the value of your other stats, as will other factors such as set bonuses. (Specifically, Critical Strike plummets in value with the class trinket off of Archimonde.) Therefore, the best way to determine what your relative stat values are is to use a tool such as Shadowcraft to obtain your values. Shadowcraft will also recommend enchanting and gemming if you so choose. Trust in Shadowcraft!

    You can also use Mr. Robot, which I've found recently is giving similar results to Shadowcraft. However, Mr. Robot is also based off of static values, so you should always trust Shadowcraft over it. You can modify the values of Mr. Robot, if you feel the need to. I often find myself using both of these and comparing the results.

    The most important thing to do, however, is to trust your better judgement. These tools are not infallible. If you get strange results from either one, at the very least ask the Rogue forums about your results. Generally, Shadowcraft will be correct, but it's better safe than 500g gold lost because you already changed your gems/enchants.

    Your Cunning - Common Tips, Tricks and Strategems (J5)

    Rotation

    Listen up!

    Single Target:
    Keep Rupture up and use Envenom when you have spare CPs. Try to avoid clipping Envenom unless there is less than 2 seconds left on it and Rupture unless there are 7 seconds left on it. Use Mutilate to build CPs, or Dispatch if you have a Blindside proc or the target is below 35%. Use Vendetta on CD as long as you can stick to the target for the majority of Vendetta's rotation. (If you're using Shadow Reflection, make sure to read the below sections.)

    Hungry for more? Read on, brothers and sisters in the shadows...


    Rotation is the common term for what abilities you should use and when. It's a bit of a relic from the days before Wrath of the Lich King, as before then there were very few, if any, ability procs such as Blindside, so doing damage mostly meant hitting a set number of buttons over and over again in the same order. Nowadays, DPS classes mostly follow a priority system. The priority system is as follows:

    Maintain 5 CP Rup > Envenom

    There are a few things to note here. It's better to have any kind of Rupture up than no Rupture. If you can, only use 5 CP Rups, but if you lapse in keeping it up and you have no energy, no Rup and 3 CPs or less, go ahead and Rup. You run the risk of not getting energy returns from Relentless Strikes, but it's better than languishing for 3-10 seconds because you have no Venomous Wounds procs and those procs account for pretty much all of your energy returns. If you have 3+ CPs and will soon have the energy for another Mutilate or Dispatch, you may want to wait though. It's one of those instinctual moments. The best solution, however, is to just watch Rupture carefully and make sure it doesn't fall off.

    It's important to note that with the new buff/debuff refreshing mechanics introduced for all classes in 6.0.2 that clipping Envenom or Rupture will extend it up to 130% of its duration. This means that you can clip a 5 CP Envenom with another 5 CP Envenom when there's only 2 seconds left (Technically, it's 1.8 seconds) or a 5 CP Rupture with another 5 CP Rupture when there's only 7 seconds left (Technically, it's 7.2 seconds) and not lose any damage.

    If you took either Nightstalker or Shadow Focus for your level 15 talent choices, then Vanish becomes a mini-CD as it offers you a free cast or increased damage every minute, assuming it's been glyphed to be one minute at least. With Shadow Reflection, use Mutilate when you Vanish. With Nightstalker, use Envenom if possible. Preparation also becomes a sort of damage cooldown since it resets the CD on Vanish so you can Prep immediately after using Vanish to get a bit more damage out of your rotation. Note that using Prep, especially if your Vanish is glyphed to be a minute CD, is a very small DPS gain, but even so, it's still a DPS gain. It's up to you to decide if the extra DPS is worth losing an instant reset on your threat/combat drop and defensive cooldowns.

    Vendetta should be used on cooldown whenever you have a situation where you can stick to the boss for the majority of the duration of Vendetta, preferably 14 seconds at least. If you're using the glyph, this window extends to 24 seconds. Make sure to pool energy before and combo points before casting Vendetta. If you're going to use your energy and combo points (Which you will.) anyways, then you might as well use them after the boss starts taking extra damage. Just make sure not to energy cap or you'll start losing out on effective DPS rather than continue gaining. After using Vendetta, make sure your first cast afterwards is Envenom, to make the most of the guaranteed crit.

    To go a bit more in depth about Vendetta usage, the idea is that the sooner you can use Vendetta, the sooner you can gain the benefit of it and it can come off CD again. Every second you spend with Vendetta not on cooldown is effectively wasted DPS. The trick is to maximize Vendetta uptime by recognizing the moments where you can gain the most benefit without waiting too long to use it. It's all about balancing this wasted time where Vendetta is not active with the time you potentially spend off the boss when Vendetta is active. If it sounds complicated, don't worry too much about it. Just follow the simple rules above!

    A Note on Shadow Reflection:
    If you're using Shadow Reflection, there's a strict method to which you should execute your cooldowns and abilities in order to maximize the damage from Shadow Reflection. First and foremost, make sure Vendetta is off cooldown. Build up a supply of combo points and energy before activating Shadow Reflection. After activated Shadow Reflection, immediately hit Vendetta followed by an Envenom. Then you need to get a 5 combo point Rupture up as soon as possible. The reasoning behind this methodology is that Shadow Reflection copies all offensive ability usage, including stuns and, more notably, Vendetta. This means your Reflection will have it's own personal Vendetta the moment it starts to attack. This is followed by Envenom for the guaranteed Crit off of improved Vendetta. Lastly, we Rupture because the Rupture will maintain even after the Reflection fades, giving you more damage even after the effect ends. After you Rupture, just follow through with your regular rotation. Assuming you pooled energy and combo points adequately beforehand, you should be able to dish out quite a bit of damage within those 8 seconds.

    Note that at high Mastery levels, such as those gained from a proccing trinket or from Mastery stacking, a third Envenom is better than using Rupture during Shadow Reflection.


    Building combo points is simple:

    Dispatch (Target below 35%, Blindside proc) > Mutilate

    For opening out of Stealth, you should be opening with Mutilate. Why Mutilate? Mutilate has double the chance to apply poisons, costs less energy than Ambush and can proc Dispatch, so Ambush is the weaker option in this case.

    Listen up!

    Multiple Targets:
    For 1-4 targets, continue with your single target rotation, cycling Rupture on the targets. Use Crimson Tempest if you can without allowing Envenom to fall off. For 5+ targets, replace your CP generator with Fan of Knives. Maintain Rupture on as many targets as possible. Alternate between Envenom and Crimson Tempest for your finishers when there's nothing left to Rupture, trying to keep both up. For burst AoE situations, use Fan of Knives and Envenom.

    Hungry for more? Read on, brothers and sisters in the shadows...


    When you're engaging more than 1-4 targets, your rotation stays more or less the same. You're going to be maintaining Rupture on 2-3 targets. This will give you more than enough energy to spam Mutilates, Dispatches and Envenoms, plus extra damage through Venomous Wounds. Use Crimson Tempest if Envenom is still up and you have the CPs. Envenom will be your priority, however.

    For 5+ targets, use Fan of Knives as your combo point generator and continue to Rupture as many enemies as you can. If there's nothing left to Rupture, switch to Crimson Tempest and Envenom as your finishers. Your priority will be Crimson Tempest; keep that up over Envenom. The reasoning behind this is that Venomous Wounds will give you more energy but will also cause quite a bit of damage on it's own. Crimson Tempest is your priority as a finisher once your Ruptures are up due to the Enhanced Crimson Tempest perk, which increases poison damage against affected enemies. This includes Deadly Poison and Venomous Wounds, which really ramps up the pain.

    For burst AoE situations (Situations where 5+ targets will be dying in 10-15 seconds.) don't bother with spreading Rupture, as it won't have enough time to make much of an impact. Just focus on Fan of Knives, Envenom and Crimson Tempest. Assuming everything does die in 10-15 seconds, your energy won't be much of an issue, especially if you're using Venom Rush. Your priority finisher in this situation is Envenom as it will net you more burst damage due to the increased application rate of Deadly Poison.
