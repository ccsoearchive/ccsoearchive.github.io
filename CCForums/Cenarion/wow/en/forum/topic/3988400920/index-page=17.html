02/02/2012 03:41 PMPosted by Zarhym
Having said all that, yesterday we discussed low-population and faction-imbalanced realms with our developers. They have some pretty bold and spectacular plans for addressing this in anticipation of implementing some of the features we plan to in Mists. I just don't have a lot of information to share with you at this stage of programming and development.


Warning: wall of text ahead.

I really want to see what Blizzard is going to attempt. Pardon me if I don't put much faith in Blizzard solving those problems, but they have been the bane of a number of MMOs out there - it's not just a WoW problem - and the ways it can be permanently solved are somewhat more extreme than what I think Blizzard is willing to try.

A permanent solution to the low realm population problem (as well as potential login queues) would most likely involve enabling players to go where other players already are, either by removing all barriers and making everyone play in a humongous sized server (perhaps using dynamic instancing to reduce the number of visible characters to a manageable number), or by allowing players to easily transfer between servers in a fast and free way.

A number of MMOs already do this:
- EVE has a single shard design; everyone plays in the same "realm".
- Rift allows players an unlimited number of free character transfers on a somewhat short cooldown (I believe it's 3 days).
- Games like CO, STO, or GW use a single shard + dynamically instanced zones (in WoW terms, it's as if the number of "realms" changed according to the number of logged players, and players could change "realms" instantly and for free).
- Games like DCUO, and in the future apparently also AoC and TSW, go a step further: players are sent to different server "instances" based on which kind of activity they want to do. DCUO has separate spaces for PvP and PvE players (with players able to change between them at will in their headquarters - akin to a WoW player being able to port to Org or SW and instantly change between PvP and PvE reals), while AoC (and, by extension, TSW, which will use the same engine) seems to be doing a "virtual servers" scheme where all players are really on the same server, but are tagged as being part of separate "virtual servers" just to keep an illusion of community; it's expected that after the move to virtual servers is complete, AoC players will be able to change their virtual server at will (besides a few exception, such as the hardcore PvP servers), which means a player might hop into a RP "virtual server" to do some RP, jump into a PvP one for a bit of world PvP, and go to a PvE one in order to catch a PUG, without having to wait or pay for the transfers.

WoW might be able to do something similar, but it would mean mixing the community and economy of all realms, making names unique game-wide (as opposed to realm-wide, like it's today; this would most likely require Blizzard to make surnames available in order to allow for enough good unique names for the whole player base), and relinquishing the character transfer revenue stream.

Faction balance is even more thorny. More so if the ways to fix low population realms are considered, since allowing players to go wherever they want will most likely make the faction balance situation worse.

Allowing players to just move between realms, without tackling faction balance, would most likely result in most realms becoming single-faction ones - players prefer to win than to fight, and joining a realm without opposition is the surest way to win. Just look at the new Brazilian realms; they all ended up heavily imbalanced, thanks to free character migration. Even players that would otherwise want a fair fight end joining the dominating faction, simply because being the whipping dog is no fun.

Doing some sort of full merge, with the servers autonomously deciding where to send each player, would make the current realm-based imbalances into global ones, with the potential to kill one of the factions - the problem DCUO is tackling right now, where Villains are quite rare game-wide, due to having done this kind of full merge without doing anything to fix faction imbalance first.

The ways I can perceive to solve faction imbalance (without forcing players to pick specific factions) involve both dynamically awarded benefits to the least powerful faction (to make players actually prefer to be in the underpowered faction) and making it easier for players to change factions, even if only temporarily (so the players that don't care about which faction they play are actually able to move between factions to boost faction population/power where it's needed). Rift is toying with this by allowing players to go "mercenary" (i.e., play for the opposite faction while receiving increased rewards), CoH allows players to change factions through in-game quests (while providing two "gray" steps that allow players to do content for both factions), GW:Factions make faction choice a guild-based one while allowing players easy ways to fight for the opposite faction, etc.

For instanced content, with the same number of players on each side, even lower queue times alone might be enough benefit if changing factions is easy and fast - it's why TF2, with it's two factions, has no problem after all; players want to be in the least played faction to get smaller queue times, and changing factions is just a click away.

All of this go against WoW's intent to keep players engaged in a single faction, to promote some in-game animosity between the factions; thus, I'm not sure Blizzard would be willing to try any of those. But I don't really see any way to solve faction imbalance without dragging down the barriers between factions; after all, for the factions to even out, players need to leave the dominating faction and join the smaller one.

If Blizzard has some answer to low population realms that don't involve either some kind of merges with dynamic instancing, or else free character transfers; and an answer to faction balance that does not involve faction benefits for the least powerful faction and easier time changing factions; I really want to see it, because I can't think of different ways to tackle this problem in a permanent way.

(I would like if Blizzard did attempt some of those solutions, though; it would show that Blizzard is willing to sacrifice old core concepts of their game to make for a better overall player experience. I don't know about other players, but between game concept integrity and fun, I will choose fun every time.)
