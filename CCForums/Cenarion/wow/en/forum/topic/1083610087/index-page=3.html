Generally, I think premarital sex would likely be looked down upon within the church of the light. However, it's my understanding that the light is meant to supply the WoW universe with a form of monotheism. As far as I know, all monotheistic faiths advice against this behavior, but acknowledge that it's not the worst thing a person could do as it's a sin of passion, rather than a sin of pre-plotted evil. I vaguely recall a quote concerning this, but forget who said it:

It's better to be a harlot before the throne of God than a lawyer, but best to be neither.

Anyway, I'm the sort of person who believes that morality has utility. I think that monotheistic faiths generally hounded premarital sex so hard because it's so easy for people to do. We're addicted to pleasure, and sex is very pleasurable. Moreover, carelessness with it has often lead to the unplanned pregnancy (I've known many people whose lives unexpectedly changed because of it). And, finally, marriage has long been an element that brings order to society. In many early cultures, bastard children were woefully common, which lead to a constant population of riffraff. Cultures that instituted a firm foundation of marriage did so to successfully cut back on this undesirable element. Premarital sex, then, is seen as a challenge to this institution and negatively stigmatized.

How this all translates into WoW could vary. Throwing magic into the mix allows for anything to happen, really. Magical contraceptives are possible, cutting back on many potential problems. However, as with many pleasurable activities, feeding it could only make the desire grow stronger, and weaken one's resolve against it. For a paladin, you'd likely want to avoid this.

Also to note, D&D Paladin's did not have to be celibate. They were expected to follow a code of conduct, and to worship their deity, but consensual relationships were allowed. The code of conduct had slight differences between first edition and fourth, as well as the various 'splat' books, but celibacy wasn't included. I read a bit of it. It's a bit confusing for me as I had always understood a religion as being a body of philosophies. As far as I can tell, the article seems to assume that a religion must be defined as having a deity figure of some kind. However, there are religions that lack a diety, such as Mahayana Buddhism.

Moreover, even common religions have complicated theologies where deity figures are understood as representing a concept. According to Judeo-Christian tradition (and I assume Muslim faiths have a similar idea), God himself is supposed to be understood as being the embodiment of good as opposed simply being an entity that has good qualities. This was more the vein of what I understood the Light as being in WoW. It's literally the single source of all goodness in the universe.

This is similar to what was suggested in that article, but not entirely the same. The article seemed to levy the importance of the Light heavily on emotional experience and feelings. This ends up lending itself more to a narcissistic approach of religion, where "if it feels good, do it" seems to apply. It felt good to give into lust, so Arthas slept with Jaina. It felt good to strike down the scourge with righteous indignation, so Arthas razed Stratholme and its people then headed to Northrend. The heart is a strong motivating force indeed, but it's more of a vehicle than a driver. A disciplined mind is needed to direct it, which runs contrary to the notion that your feelings are the soul dictator of your understanding of the Light.
