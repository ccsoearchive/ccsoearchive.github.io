Um, I'm not sure what you mean. Druids use the same combat table, it's just that they can't parry or block, so those are both 0%. So for you, against a level 85 mob, it'd be:

Miss...7% (Extra 2% for being a night elf)
Dodge..25.04%
Parry..0%
Block..0%
Hit....67.96%

Because druids (And DKs) can't block, it's impossible to push that hit chance to 0, so it's not something they worry about.

Basically, the more dodge rating you get, the less dodge chance you get per point of dodge rating, and the more parry rating you get, the less parry chance you get per point of parry rating.

So, if you have 0 dodge rating, that's 5% dodge chance (you've only got your base 5% chance to dodge). If you get 100 dodge rating, that's 5.59% dodge chance. 100 dodge rating has given you an extra 0.59% chance to dodge.

If you have 1000 dodge rating, that's 10.43% dodge chance. If you get 1100 dodge rating, that's 10.92% dodge chance. 100 dodge rating has given you an extra 0.49% chance to dodge, less than if you go from 0 to 100 dodge chance.

If you have 2000 dodge rating, that's 15.03% dodge chance. If you get 2100 dodge rating, that's 15.45% dodge chance. 100 dodge rating has given you an extra 0.42% chance to dodge, still less than if you go from 0 to 100, or 1000 to 1100.


Which isn't to say that too much dodge and parry is bad, it just makes it so too much dodge and parry isn't absurdly good. Going from 98% to 99% dodge is much much better than going from 0% to 1% dodge, because you're avoiding half the attacks that were hitting you before, while if you go from 0% to 1% to dodge, you're only avoiding 1% more of the attacks. Without diminishing returns, going from 98% to 99% dodge would require the same amount of dodge rating as 0% to 1% dodge, despite it being much more powerful. It also makes it so that it's no longer possible to make a character that can never be hit by melee attacks.
