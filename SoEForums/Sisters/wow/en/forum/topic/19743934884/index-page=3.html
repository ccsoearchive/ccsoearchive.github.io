--------------------------------------------------------------

Topic 3: Alienation of the Self

Greetings, I welcome both the familiar and new faces. This week marks our third gathering in honour of the Shadow and with the growing interest of this project comes the evolution of the event. Unlike past gatherings which have focused on the sharing of knowledge to the largest of audiences, beginning tonight we shall focus on a more intimate setting. As in line with the teachings of the Shadow we will strive for engagement. As such, the speaking floor that has been a prominent feature in past meetings shall now be of less importance. In its place I will instead ask each of you, at the appropriate time, to form small groups with your peers gathered here tonight-- to share your knowledge and have your understandings tested. If this structure of lecture and seminar is successful you may expect it as the norm when attending these Sermons in the future.

Now then, I will speak on the topic of the evening: Alienation of the Self. I ask that each of you consider my words; let them be a basis for your own understanding and arguments.

We’ve spoken before on the nature of existence being one of self-actualization and self-interpretation. Today we are going to carry those concepts further and discuss the -way- of being, that is, how the self interacts within the world and how the world interacts with the self. The first concept I will pose to you is the idea of two selves existing as one whole-- What I mean by this is that each of us is characterized not only by our own interpretation of ourselves, that is what we consider to be our identity, but also characterized by the objectification of our self through the eyes of the Other, that is the way our peers may interpret us. It is important to understand that these are not two separate aliases existing in opposition of each other but rather two pieces that come together to form a whole.

This idea may explain the feelings of the uncanny, where one does not feel at home within the world. This feeling of alienation is caused by the fact that we realize that we are not the revealers of the world, insofar as the world is not a representation of us, and by extension the ‘Other’s’ that inhabit the world are also foreign. This feeling of uneasiness, the uncanny, then comes about as we realize that the total sum of what we may be is not just a matter of our own Will, our own interpretation of ourself, or our own self-making but is in instead revealed, in part, through the world and the Will of others. That is to say, each of us does not merely exist as a being-for-ourself but a being-for-others.

To elaborate on this concept further; when we are engaged in a task that allows us to be unreflective, we are nothing but the first-person perspective of ourselves. We are absorbed in our practice and do not experience ourselves as possessing an “outside”, that is, we do not perceive or understand our actions and behaviour in the capacity of the third-person. However, when we become aware that we are being observed, that is to say, when we become aware that our subjectivity is being invaded by the subjectivity of another we are made aware of our “character”. To explain further, in the eyes of the Other we are no longer the sum of what we interpret ourselves to be, but rather, in the interpretation of the Other we simply exist as objects within the world; tools for the Other to use as a means-to-an-end. It is in this state of awareness to the subjectivity of the Other and the objectification of ourself that we become privy to having a “nature”, a “character” of being.

To simplify this concept I will use myself as an example: I am not merely speaking and sharing my knowledge; I am perhaps an educator, a radical, or even a fool. In short, the mode, or the way, in which my being is recognized is through the subjectivity of all of you gathered here. I cannot experience myself in the way of the third person, as being -something-. Only you, the Other, can identity this mode of my being and I may do nothing but acknowledge it as mine. Yet there is a shame in this acknowledgement because it requires me to recognize that it is only through the Other that I may understand the third-person perspective on myself. This act in itself reveals the extent to which I am alienated from the formation of my being. Who I am, in an objective sense, can only be revealed through the perspective and interpretation of the Other. Therefore, it is only through the other that Self can be thought of as whole.

Finally, our practices, those actions which we pursue as a result of our WIll and that bring meaning to the world for us, do not inherently belong to us. The ownership of those actions belong to the world, from the tradition or society in which we find ourselves. Furthermore, how we perceive our practices is grounded in the foundation of various social norms. For instance; if my practice is to spread knowledge, and to the other I am interpreted as an educator than I am only successful in my practice if what I do aligns with the Others understanding of what it means to -be- a good educator. You may find this logic in many things: A knife is only successful in relation to how well it cuts. A shield is only successful in relation to how well it protects, and so on. The conclusion of this thought is that what I do, my practices, must be done in a “normal” way in order to be recognizable. As such, I am essentially interchangeable with anyone else who does my practice; as the success in which my practice is measured is based upon what is defined as average in my society, culture, and so on.

Thus, if who we are is defined through existing, and the nature of our existence is brought about through our practices, and our practices can only be found successful in relation to predefined norms; then it follows that the Self we identity as belonging to us is thereby mostly indistinguishable from the Other. In other words, we are essentially anonymous or “anyone”.

And so it is for these reasons that we experience the alienation of the Self and why it may be thought that our very engagement within the world limits our ability to be the “authentic” version of ourself.

Now, I ask of you all to arrange yourselves into small groups and discuss the topic. Agree or disagree with what I have said here tonight, discuss it and debate it. Consider times in your own life where you have felt this alienation, or reflect on situations and theories which may dispute the knowledge I have shared this evening. How you approach your group discussions is entirely up to you. Now, go and engage.

"The world is not always so shadow and light. We have seen followers of the light use their powers unjustly and those who wield the shadows for doing good. No matter what sort of fount you draw your powers from, no matter what forces you harness with precision, or even what faith you have in your own abilities, it is what you decide to do with any of these that indicates how evil or good you are. In of it's self the Shadow is neither good or evil. Those amoung the priest class who wield the Shadow draw their power not from a force which is inherently good or evil, they draw from the void it's self which is the very stuff all things good and evil reside.

To embrace the Shadow is not to embrace the void and the line of insanity which shadow priests tread. It is to embrace a way of learning about your existence, adapting to the realities in this existence created by the will of you and others, and a way to transcend the boundaries both seen and unseen in our existence. This is what the Shadow means to me."
