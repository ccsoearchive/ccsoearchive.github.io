Said additions were part of trying to make the system a little more friendly for certain things, and giving people less chance for failure at things that their character should be good at. Bonuses are applied at the base level, so a roll of 1 on something your character is good at would not be a critical failure. The DC of many checks are set by whoever's doing the leading of the event.

The main reason for the roll system itself was a couple of problems that the event leaders ran into in previous events before WoF:


  • Characters playing ridiculous gods and getting into over-dramatic posturing in every fight because... reasons, I guess
  • Large, freeform events becoming such a mess trying to keep track of who was doing what and having 5-15 people all screaming about how they were landing the killing blow on something that had apparently been dead for six posts according to three of the characters who suffered from the above problem.


Rolls in combat are to determine who of the group attacks successfully this round, and helps the event leaders keep track of whose posts they need to look for and respond to. It's then the event leader's job to keep tabs on people who might be rolling low during the fight and having the opponents focus on them a little, to ensure that everyone stays engaged.

Outside of combat things are generally a little more open to whatever the player wants to do. If it's a somewhat story-driven event then we might have specific details that are tied to a successful roll of X/Y/Z. It allows us to pre-plan a little, write up some descriptions of items they find, people they meet, or encounters they might... encounter. But in the end, it's not meant to boil down to wholly dice roll-driven plot.

Some examples:
  • At the end of a recent story, the party was going through the loot. Everyone was asked to roll, adding Treasure Hunter if they had the trait. While everyone still got loot, the person who rolled the highest found an item of particular interest.
  • About a month back, the group came across a wagon that had been rocket-propelled into the cliffside across from the road. While I'd set up a couple options to use climbing rolls to get up to it or strength to try to grapple it and pull it down, one of our druids just flew up and bear formed onto it, dropping down and trying to use their weight to knock it to the ground.


With larger numbers (Say, a plain /roll system) you have more balancing issues to deal with, and with smaller numbers, you have a higher chance of critically failing due to the smaller granularity. d20 is also fairly familiar, which should make it easier for people to pick up than a completely new system.

This all said, I didn't design the original system. I suggested the Trait system and have been working to try to help balance that out with it. Any system I'd design would end up so complex and convoluted it likely wouldn't work within WoW's constraints.
